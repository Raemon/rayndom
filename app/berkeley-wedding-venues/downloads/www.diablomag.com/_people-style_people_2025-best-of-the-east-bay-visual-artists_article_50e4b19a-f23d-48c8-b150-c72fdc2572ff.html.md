**In recent months, an impressive number of artists with ties to the East Bay have received national attention for their thought-provoking work. Here, we celebrate three wildly different innovators who have a shared passion for challenging assumptions.**

### BRIDGING LIFE AND DEATH

![DM2507_066_DIG.jpg](data:image/png;base64,iVBORw0KGgoAAAANSUhEUgAAAAQAAAADCAQAAAAe/WZNAAAAEElEQVR42mM8U88ABowYDABAxQPltt5zqAAAAABJRU5ErkJggg==)

Angela Hennessy

Airyka Rockefeller

Oakland-based artist Angela Hennessy was one of three Bay Area creatives to receive a 2024 SECA Art Award from SFMOMA, which exhibited a selection of her pieces earlier this year. Hennessy, who creates artworks using methods associated with domestic labor—washing, stitching, braiding—produced a series of memorial wreaths made from natural and synthetic hair, drawing on cross-cultural mourning traditions. “Hair is a material that is exchanged between the living and the dead,” Hennessy said in an interview with the Joan Mitchell Foundation, where she was a fellow. “There are rituals and traditions where hair is offered as a sign of respect or as a gesture of grief when someone has died … I’m really interested in how materials can make visible or tactile these relationships between the living and the dead.”

### SEEING THE UNSEEN

![DM2507_071_DIG.jpg](data:image/png;base64,iVBORw0KGgoAAAANSUhEUgAAAAQAAAADCAQAAAAe/WZNAAAAEElEQVR42mM8U88ABowYDABAxQPltt5zqAAAAABJRU5ErkJggg==)

Trevor Paglen

Christine Jones

Multidisciplinary artist Trevor Paglen has long strived to make the invisible visible. Intrigued by what he calls a landscape of surveillance, he has photographed, filmed, and written about secret military installations, aerospace technology, spy satellites, undersea cables, and manifestations of artificial intelligence, among other interests—all as a way to address a world we are not meant to see. His boundary-shattering projects draw upon extensive research to shed light on the hidden forces that are increasingly shaping our society. Based in New York and Berlin, Paglen received his Ph.D. in geography from UC Berkeley and in 2017 was named a MacArthur Fellow. In May, he was an honoree at BAMPFA’s annual Art and Film Benefit. “I’m interested in exploring those ‘geographies’ of secrecy from many different angles,” Paglen said in an interview with the _Verge,_ “because I am fundamentally just interested in how the world works.”

Ronald Rael's installation _Adobe Oasis_ at the Desert X 2025 exhibition.

### REINVENTING ANCIENT ARCHITECTURE

![DM2507_077_DIG.jpg](data:image/png;base64,iVBORw0KGgoAAAANSUhEUgAAAAQAAAADCAQAAAAe/WZNAAAAEElEQVR42mM8U88ABowYDABAxQPltt5zqAAAAABJRU5ErkJggg==)

Ronald Rael

Kate Jones

In his art, architecture, teaching, and writing, Ronald Rael explores the intersection of traditional building practices and contemporary technology. The Eva Li Memorial Chair in Architecture at UC Berkeley, Rael recently created a large site-specific installation titled _Adobe Oasis_ for this year’s Desert X exhibition in the Coachella Valley. The installation, which marries ancient earthen construction with 3D printing, is an artistic vision for how sustainable architecture can be realized through digital techniques. Rael is also the cofounder of Emerging Objects, a company with the aim of “inventing recipes that would allow us to ask questions about the future of architecture through the lens of 3D printing.” This year, Rael and design partner Virginia San Fratello received the prestigious Digital Design Award from the Cooper Hewitt, Smithsonian Design Museum.
