Kinisium

Engineer Mahboud Zabetian and his wife, Nicky, have established some serious East Bay roots. Their children have gone through Orinda schools (Sleepy Hollow Elementary and Miramonte High), they frequent Mount Diablo (Mahboud used to paraglide there), and they have created businesses such as Middleton Makes. Their latest undertaking, a wireless motion sensor called Kini that keeps an eye on your valuables, was born out of COVID times.

![DM2403_075_DIG.jpg](data:image/png;base64,iVBORw0KGgoAAAANSUhEUgAAAAQAAAADCAQAAAAe/WZNAAAAEElEQVR42mM8U88ABowYDABAxQPltt5zqAAAAABJRU5ErkJggg==)

Kini inventors Nicky and Mahboud Zabetian.

Kinisium

“We had a family dinner where we were discussing working on things that would make a difference,” says Mahboud, who immigrated to the United States from Iran in 1983, escaping his homeland during the Iran-Iraq War. “Some of the problems in the world are more than a few people can solve, but one thing that came out was that our kids had, over their high school and even elementary school years, been exposed to guns in friends’ homes. I didn’t know about this until years later and thought, _Whoa, that’s dangerous._ A gun could be taken at any time by anyone in the house. I thought, _Well, that’s something that could actually be solved._ And we came up with the idea of the Kini.”

The Kini is a small tool that can be placed wherever you are concerned that movement might occur, and you’ll get a text in about 10 seconds if it does—it’s a way to offer peace of mind about everything from your prized _Star Wars_ action figures to the liquor cabinet. There’s no lengthy sign-up process where a truckload of personal information is shared; your cell phone number does the trick.

![DM2403_073_DIG.jpg](data:image/png;base64,iVBORw0KGgoAAAANSUhEUgAAAAQAAAADCAQAAAAe/WZNAAAAEElEQVR42mM8U88ABowYDABAxQPltt5zqAAAAABJRU5ErkJggg==)

Valuable items such as golf clubs can benefit from an attached Kini.

Kinisium

“It doesn’t involve a third party,” says Nicky. “You’re the only one getting that information, from someone rummaging in your closet or peeking at the Christmas presents that you hid. We have heard stories of people who have the combinations to safes or their stashes of marijuana discovered, and if they didn’t have that notification, they would never have known. The Kini is not meant to thwart professional thieves, but it can help make sure your family and people invited into your home are not accessing things they shouldn’t be.”

While Mahboud has handled the engineering side of Kini, his wife has been right by his side overseeing the marketing. “When I was a kid, I used to think if you built a better mousetrap, they’d be beating down your door,” Mahboud says. “But nobody just comes to your door. Nicky has to find paths to connections. Her role is essential, and I don’t know what I would do without her.” _[kinisium.com](http://kinisium.com)._
