A glance at the name **Volcanic Legacy Scenic Byway** is enough to bring up visions of conical peaks, steaming fumaroles, and expanses of black lava. And indeed, all of those features and more define the surreal landscapes along this multipronged route, which spiderwebs across a swath of northeastern California and southern Oregon so rich in natural wonders that it’s protected by a half dozen national and state parks. And that number just got bigger with the designation of **Sáttítla Highlands National Monument** in January 2025, the end of a long battle by the Pit River, Modoc, and other Indigenous groups to protect sacred ancestral lands.

The tiny village of **McCloud,** a serene cluster of 19th-century clapboard storefronts and bungalows backdropped by Mount Shasta, makes a centrally located home base to explore the area. One of the last company towns in the United States, it was owned by the McCloud River Lumber Company until the late 1960s. It remains a quiet backwater where fishing, boating, and outdoor recreation are the main topics of conversation in the shops and cafés now housed in **McCloud River Mercantile Company,** the block-long former company store.

![DM2507_090_DIG.jpg](data:image/png;base64,iVBORw0KGgoAAAANSUhEUgAAAAQAAAADCAQAAAAe/WZNAAAAEElEQVR42mM8U88ABowYDABAxQPltt5zqAAAAABJRU5ErkJggg==)

The clear waters of Medicine Lake are ideal for swimming and boating.

Courtesy of Discover Siskiyou 

It’s around an hour drive from McCloud to the nearly 225,000-acre Sáttítla Highlands, which contains the majority of the **Medicine Lake Volcano,** prized for its caldera lake, acres of jet-black lava shield, and glass flows of glittering obsidian. One of the best ways to see this phenomenon up close is to hike Glass Mountain, where a trail meanders through piles of knife-sharp rock. (Be sure to wear closed-toe hiking shoes.)

Surrounded by pine forests and with several wide, sandy beaches, Medicine Lake is a popular spot for swimming, fishing, and boating. Several campgrounds dot the lakefront, where the slope is gentle enough that kayakers can put in from the shore. Rent a kayak from **McCloud Outdoors and Gear Exchange,** and the friendly staff will help you load it on your car and even offer evening returns if you favor a sunset paddle.

![Lava Beds National monument in USA](data:image/png;base64,iVBORw0KGgoAAAANSUhEUgAAAAQAAAADCAQAAAAe/WZNAAAAEElEQVR42mM8U88ABowYDABAxQPltt5zqAAAAABJRU5ErkJggg==)

Lava Beds National Monument has arresting subterranean scenery.

Courtesy of Shasta Cascade Wonderland Association

Twisting and climbing past Mammoth Crater, the Volcanic Legacy Scenic Byway narrows to a well-graded gravel road before dipping down into **Lava Beds National Monument,** an eerie landscape of prickly black lava fields under-laced by a labyrinth of more than 800 caves and tunnels.

Most people test their tolerance for caving at Mushpot Cave, just behind the visitor’s center, which has been outfitted with a raised paved pathway, signage, and electric lights. Unless claustrophobia or physical limitations intervene, pick up a cave permit and map highlighting the major caves, which are helpfully described and classified in terms of difficulty. You’ll need a headlamp and flashlight to go into the caves, which are even darker than you might imagine. Ancient pictographs adorn several of the cave entrances, with the best examples at Symbol Bridge and Big Painted Cave.

A dark and little-remembered chapter in California and Oregon history comes into vivid relief at Captain Jack’s Stronghold, the site where fewer than 60 Modoc warriors led by Kintpuash, more commonly known as Captain Jack, held off as many as 1,000 U.S. troops during the Modoc War of 1872–73. Here, a lava-encrusted hill provided a natural fortress where the band—which totaled about 150 people, including families and children—held out for almost six freezing months in the unforgiving terrain.

![DM2507_085_DIG.jpg](data:image/png;base64,iVBORw0KGgoAAAANSUhEUgAAAAQAAAADCAQAAAAe/WZNAAAAEElEQVR42mM8U88ABowYDABAxQPltt5zqAAAAABJRU5ErkJggg==)

Birders will love Tule Lake National Wildlife Refuge.

Courtesy of Discover Siskiyou 

The route to the monument’s northern entrance skims the edge of **Tule Lake National Wildlife Refuge,** a major flyway stop for migratory birds, where the waters are awash with wood ducks, grebes, and red-winged blackbirds. Be on the alert for bald and golden eagles and possibly even pronghorn antelope stopping by for a drink.

* * *

### Time Travels

Stepping into the McCloud River Mercantile Company brings a feeling of history on all sides, from the astonishingly thick, deep-grained floorboards beneath your feet to the walls adorned with black-and-white photos documenting the town’s lumber-infused past.

![DM2507_084_DIG.jpg](data:image/png;base64,iVBORw0KGgoAAAANSUhEUgAAAAQAAAADCAQAAAAe/WZNAAAAEElEQVR42mM8U88ABowYDABAxQPltt5zqAAAAABJRU5ErkJggg==)

Downtown McCloud is on the National Register of Historic Places.

Courtesy of Discover Siskiyou 

Occupying most of the upper level and a few ground-floor rooms, the **McCloud Mercantile Hotel** celebrates its past with interiors that balance artful restoration, vintage chic, and contemporary comforts. The rooms feature gleaming pine floors, tall windows, and ornate chandeliers and furnishings. Guests have access to a centrally located kitchen and parlor stocked with games.

The town’s central gathering place, the **Butcher Block Grill,** takes its name from its location in the mercantile’s old meat market, and you can still spot original touches like the heavy steel meat locker door. A collection of historic photos and old lumber company signs with messages like "Danger, Timber Falling Area" and "Danger, Blasting" nod to bygone times, as do the menu items such as rib eye and New York steak. The fare ventures beyond the meat-market theme to include pecan-crusted salmon, chicken piccata, and several pasta dishes enhanced by salads and fluffy, fresh-baked rolls.

Breakfast options in town include the **White Mountain Café** and **Sojourn,** adjacent to McCloud Outdoors and Gear Exchange, which features a robust menu including quiche that tastes like it comes straight from a Paris market. **Bogey’s Café** serves hearty breakfast and lunch fare at the **McCloud Golf Club;** established in 1923, it is one of the oldest continuously operating nine-hole courses in California.

* * *

### Dark Skies Ahead

Heading southeast from McCloud, the Volcanic Legacy Scenic Byway encircles **Lassen Volcanic National Park,** with massive dormant volcano Lassen Peak dominating the skyline.

While Lassen Volcanic National Park merits a multiday trip on its own, the main park drive takes you past most major highlights. Hot spots include Lassen Peak and its Devastated Area cinder fields, the eerie rock piles known as Jumbles, and bubbling hydrothermal sites like Sulphur Works and Bumpass Hell.

Almost entirely devoid of light pollution, Lassen boasts some of the inkiest skies in the continental U.S. Stargazing tips are available at the visitor’s centers.

Plan to stay in the park past sunset on a moonless night, and the vision of the Milky Way reflected in the still waters of the Summit, Helen, or Manzanita lakes will remain with you far beyond the drive home.
