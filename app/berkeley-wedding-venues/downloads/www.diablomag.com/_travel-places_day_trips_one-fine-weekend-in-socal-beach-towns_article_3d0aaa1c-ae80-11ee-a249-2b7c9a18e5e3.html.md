Redondo Beach Tourism

On chilly winter days, the mind drifts to Hawaii, Cabo San Lucas, or other tropical getaways. But it’s hard to pass up the convenience of a short flight from Oakland International Airport (OAK) to Los Angeles International Airport (LAX) to visit Los Angeles County’s South Bay beach towns—**Redondo Beach, Hermosa Beach,** and **Manhattan Beach** —each one under an hour’s drive from LAX. Unlike NorCal’s windswept beaches, SoCal’s milder Mediterranean climate makes beach-going an option on most days year-round.

The beauty of a weekend getaway in Redondo Beach is that after a mere one-hour-plus flight (departing almost hourly from Oakland on Southwest) and then an Uber or taxi ride to your hotel, you don’t have to set foot in a vehicle for the rest of your stay. That’s because most everywhere worth visiting is within easy walking distance—or pedaling distance if you wish to check out the smaller neighboring beach towns of Hermosa and Manhattan by bike. Two more reasons to visit in the off-season: Redondo’s hotel rates are lower, and the crowds are always thinner than in L.A. County’s more famous beach towns—Santa Monica, Venice, and Malibu. It’s the perfect car-free coastal escape.

### Shore Stays

Redondo Beach’s handful of waterfront hotels (along with most of the best restaurants and activity options) are clustered just north of the horseshoe-shaped **Redondo Pier,** the heartbeat of town. **Shade Hotel** is a top choice, with a rooftop pool and views of King Harbor and the ocean beyond. The **Portofino Hotel and Marina** has equally stellar vistas from its guest rooms and 20-foot-high windows in the elegantly redesigned **Living Room and Craft Cocktail Bar. Sonesta Redondo Beach and Marina,** the city’s biggest hotel, features a large pool, a tennis court, and balconies with marina views from most of the 342 rooms. The Hilton Tapestry Collection’s **Redondo Beach Hotel** is the most affordable of the premier hotels, with winter weekend rates under $200, while still offering a pool and seascapes.

Shake off any travel stress after checking into your hotel and change into your beach attire. It’s only a short walk to the wide sandy beach immediately south of Redondo Pier, where you can observe the surfers and beach volleyball players as the Palos Verdes Peninsula rises ahead in the distance.

### Get a Move On

The nonstop parade of locals and visitors on bikes on the **Strand,** the recreational path that weaves through Redondo Pier, is evidence that two-wheel travel is wildly popular in the area. Even if you haven’t cycled in decades, it doesn’t get any easier than a spin on a fat-tire cruiser or e-bike on the paved multiuse path that hugs the beach for most of the 20 miles north to Pacific Palisades. Guests can get complimentary e-bikes at the Portofino Hotel’s **Dockside Adventure Center** for a pedal-assisted ride, but those content with a beach cruiser or city bike for the trek to Hermosa and Manhattan may pick one up at Redondo’s **Marina Bike Rentals** ($12 an hour). It’s a great kick-back activity, as the Strand crosses no streets—you won’t see a car for miles—and the people-watching and hypnotic thrum of the waves 50 yards from the path keep the ride from ever getting monotonous.

Make two stops as you head north. At **Hermosa Beach Pier,** check out the surfer scene along the pedestrian-only block of surf shops, restaurants, and bars. To keep the vibe going, pedal a few blocks inland to the **Hermosa Beach Museum** (open from 2 to 4 p.m.). It’s free, and you’ll learn all about the town’s rich surfing history.

![DM2401_017_DIG.jpg](data:image/png;base64,iVBORw0KGgoAAAANSUhEUgAAAAQAAAADCAQAAAAe/WZNAAAAEElEQVR42mM8U88ABowYDABAxQPltt5zqAAAAABJRU5ErkJggg==)

Sun, sand, and sports abound at Redondo Beach.

Redondo Beach Tourism

Next, cruise the Strand to **Manhattan Beach Pier** and watch surfers weave through the waves before you duck into the **Roundhouse Aquarium,** a tiny (and free) museum at the pier’s end. Continue north on the Strand to **Dockweiler State Beach** in the next town of El Segundo to see a procession of planes fly over the beach or to sign up for a beginner’s hang-gliding lesson off a 30-foot dune with **Windsports.**

For another type of adventure, take to the water. **King Harbor** is best explored via kayak, paddleboard, or pedal boat; **Pedal Port** has rentals for $12-$15 an hour. If you’d rather tour the extensive harbor and marinas without the workout, enjoy a 40-minute ride with **Glass Bottom Boat Redondo Beach.** Whichever vessel you choose, you’re sure to see harbor seals, sea lions, and garibaldi, which resemble goldfish on steroids. January to April is the ideal time for a 2.5-hour whale-watching cruise (**Redondo Beach Sportfishing and Whale Watch** hosts tours all year).

### Pier Party

Once you have worked up an appetite, the Redondo Pier area has many restaurants that source seafood from local fishermen, and with fewer visitors in winter and spring, you’re likely to score a waterside table.

Featuring excellent marina views from most tables are **Sea Level,** which serves elevated fare such as a 40-day dry-aged New York strip steak, and **Baleen Kitchen,** with its dazzling interior, creative seafood (try the porcini-dusted scallops), and live music on weekends. **Kincaid’s** is a can’t-miss eatery right on Redondo Pier, with delectable menu items like sesame-seared ahi and applejack mahi-mahi, while **Chart House** is another fine waterside option. And if you want to refuel while you are on the move, scarf down a hearty “strandwich” at the always-bustling **Scotty’s on the Strand** in Hermosa Beach or a classic lobster roll from the **Strand House** in Manhattan Beach.

Once you’ve beach-walked, pedaled, paddled, and dined all day, the perfect nightcap is a signature mai tai at the retro **Tony’s on the Pier,** a circular bar perched atop a storied 1952 restaurant, or one of the 88 beers on tap at lively **Naja’s Place.** At either spot, you can raise a toast to the beauty of SoCal’s beaches and your relaxing winter getaway.
