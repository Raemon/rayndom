The Tower Bridge Dinner celebrates Sacramento’s culinary scene.

Francisco Chavira/Visit Sacramento

Tickets for the **Tower Bridge Dinner,** held every September in Sacramento, usually sell out within minutes. There are 80 tickets at $600 for a pair, and the people who get them consider themselves lucky. Rotating teams of all-star chefs come together to strut their stuff, showcasing locally sourced ingredients during a party that hovers over the Sacramento River.

While the dinner is just one big night, foodies around the country are taking notice of the city’s extraordinary dining offerings available year-round—a culinary scene that embraces a food ethos of farm to fork.

Chef Patrick Mulvaney, who helped found the Tower Bridge Dinner in 2013, has become an ambassador for Sacramento’s food world. Speaking from **Mulvaney’s B and L** —his firehouse-turned-upscale-eatery—he easily identifies the city’s driving force. “It’s all about access,” Mulvaney says. “We have access to the best agriculture in the world, and that’s our advantage.”

Perhaps the restaurant currently shining the brightest is the one with the newly acquired 2023 Michelin star: **Localis**. Housed in a narrow open space in midtown, Localis has an intimacy that accentuates chef-owner Chris Barnum-Dann’s welcoming personality. The restaurant features a 12-course tasting menu that changes every five weeks. Some recent plates include green curry ceviche, California halibut with black garlic fish sauce puree, and Mariposa Ranch filet with a napoleon of roasted corn and coconut panna cotta. “It is food at the highest level, service at the highest level, and vibes at the highest level,” says Barnum-Dann.

Led by acclaimed chef Brad Cecchi, East Sacramento’s **Canon** is included in the Michelin Guide’s Bib Gourmand list of more affordable restaurants. A respected purveyor of seasonal cuisine since it opened in 2017, Canon’s varied menu of “quick bites” (mole tots, goat cheese–stuffed naan), small plates (citrus-cured hamachi, duck confit chopped salad), and platters (whole fried fish, grilled New York strip) is meant to be shared. Perch at the first-rate bar and sip a craft cocktail while nibbling on chicken drumsticks in urfa chili sauce.

Billy Ngo, **Kru Contemporary Japanese Cuisine** ’s co-owner and chef, is also earning industry accolades; he is a 2024 James Beard Awards nominee in the Best Chef: California category (winners will be announced on June 10). Sushi-centric Kru emphasizes the Japanese tradition of omakase, which trusts the chef to conceive an individual dining experience for the customer. However, don’t hesitate to ask for one of the daily specials, like lobster mushroom dumplings or Tokyo turnip soup. Ngo is a partner at several other Sacramento-based restaurants, including **Fish Face Poke Bar,** located in the hip R Street corridor. This counter service poke bar features Hawaiian- and Japanese-based sushi rolls and udon that are perfect for lunch. Ngo’s **Kodaiko Ramen and Bar** on the K Street Mall is just a block away from the Golden 1 Center, making it a good stop before seeing a Kings game or a concert. Kodaiko’s specialties are its _tonkotsu_ —a ramen bowl steeped in three-day pork _paitan_ (broth) with slices of pork belly—and kara age, delicious Japanese fried chicken.

## Regional Focus

![DM2405_026_DIG.jpg](data:image/png;base64,iVBORw0KGgoAAAANSUhEUgAAAAQAAAADCAQAAAAe/WZNAAAAEElEQVR42mM8U88ABowYDABAxQPltt5zqAAAAABJRU5ErkJggg==)

Enjoy Allora’s Conserva 3.0, featuring _‘nduja,_ mussels, and calamari.

Anna Wick

Ngo’s James Beard acknowledgment has been an inspiration for his colleagues, who appreciate the increased visibility for the area. “It’s such a huge deal that our community is getting recognized,” says Elizabeth-Rose Mandalou.

Mandalou and her husband, chef Deneb Williams, opened **Allora,** a modern Italian restaurant with a focus on Old World wines, in 2018. Surviving the early days of the pandemic gave Mandalou and Williams the confidence to switch to a prix fixe menu, which had been their dream, in 2020. The four courses include a crudos and antipasti opener with a choice of oysters, buttermilk-fried quail, or balsamic-glazed octopus. Other courses feature wild boar ravioli, lobster prawn fettuccine, short rib, and Sonoma duck. All courses are available with wine pairings, Mandalou’s specialty.

![DM2405_051_DIG.jpg](data:image/png;base64,iVBORw0KGgoAAAANSUhEUgAAAAQAAAADCAQAAAAe/WZNAAAAEElEQVR42mM8U88ABowYDABAxQPltt5zqAAAAABJRU5ErkJggg==)

Sacramento’s Allora restaurant serves a refreshing strawberry soup.

Anna Wick

Speaking to the restaurant’s move to a prix fixe menu, Mandalou says they decided to “just do what we [want] and stay true to that.”

Ed Roehr’s **Magpie** takes a similar approach. The friendly restaurant in the heart of midtown serves creative comfort food in a casual environment. Specialties include vegetable and heirloom bean soup, avocado salmon toast, and a Niman Ranch pork chop served on polenta from Grass Valley Grains.

“We’ve tried to say: What does it mean to be a cuisine in the Central Valley? How do we interact with the greater concept of California cuisine?” says Roehr. “If you don’t make seasonal food, I don’t know that you’re really reflecting the environment or the land.”

## Eyes on the Pies

The city has really raised its pizza game in the last few years with the arrival of serious newcomers **Majka** and **Pizza Supreme Being.** Majka’s distinctive crust is crafted with organically grown whole grains made with wild yeast. (Husband-and-wife owners Alex Sherry and Chutharat Sae Tong are former members of Berkeley’s Cheese Board Collective.) Initially all-vegetarian, the eatery now has a sausage pizza (ask for it if it’s not on the menu, which changes weekly). A representative seasonal pie may include an arrabbiata sauce of San Marzano tomatoes, imported mozzarella, Sicilian black olives, and morel mushrooms. Grab one of their decadent chocolate chunk miso cookies if you can; they fly out of the store.

Operating with a “punk rock attitude,” Pizza Supreme Being recently expanded its space (located near the Capitol building). Chef-owner Ben Roberts is also growing his menu of slices, squares, and whole 20-inch pies with additional pizza offerings and unique sandwiches (for example, a lamb _merguez_ sausage sandwich on a house-baked sesame seed hoagie or a smoked bacon and kale melt sandwich). Traditional favorites like pepperoni come on house-made sourdough crust topped with organic tomato sauce; Roberts swears by the Midnight Moon cheese he gets from Humboldt County and uses in his melty blend. Make sure to order ahead as the pizzas often sell out.

From singular experiences like the Tower Bridge Dinner to well-crafted casual meals, Sacramento has become an exciting and ever-evolving regional dining destination.
