![DM2405_097_DIG.jpg](data:image/png;base64,iVBORw0KGgoAAAANSUhEUgAAAAQAAAADCAQAAAAe/WZNAAAAEElEQVR42mM8U88ABowYDABAxQPltt5zqAAAAABJRU5ErkJggg==)

A selection of vintage furniture at the Eames Archives in Richmond.

Nicholas Calcott

In 2019, _The World of Charles and Ray Eames,_ featuring some 400 objects, concluded its four-month run at the Oakland Museum of California. According to Llisa Demetrios, the husband-and-wife designers’ granddaughter, the popularity of the exhibition prompted the question, “Where are we going to share this next?” Five years on, she has an answer: The nonprofit Eames Institute of Infinite Curiosity, of which she is the chief curator, recently debuted the Eames Archives, a gallery and collections space in Richmond that offers guided tours.

Among the couple’s contributions to 20th-century architecture and design are their landmark Los Angeles residence, known as Case Study House No. 8; the Sling seating used in airports worldwide; and the molded plywood Eames Lounge Chair and Ottoman. With the archives, several thousand artifacts are displayed, from personal correspondence and possessions to furniture prototypes and finished products.

![DM2405_096_DIG.jpg](data:image/png;base64,iVBORw0KGgoAAAANSUhEUgAAAAQAAAADCAQAAAAe/WZNAAAAEElEQVR42mM8U88ABowYDABAxQPltt5zqAAAAABJRU5ErkJggg==)

Llisa Demetrios, the granddaughter of legendary designers Charles and Ray Eames.

Nicholas Calcott

“We want to immerse people in their world as much as we can,” says Demetrios, who began inventorying her maternal grandparents’ trove a handful of years ago. Totaling more than 40,000 items, it is twice as large as she initially thought. While the 90-minute archives tours cover only a small fraction of the collection, Demetrios packs them with insights and anecdotes. “You can’t show everything on a subject,” she says. “All you can do is show five or six things that get someone excited enough to learn more.”

Tours start with an introduction to Charles and Ray, prior to their meeting at Cranbrook Academy of Art in Michigan. Close-up looks at fabric samples, furniture components, and chairs aplenty help illustrate “the amazing career they had together,” Demetrios says, noting that there was no separation between their home and work lives. A retail area in the lobby of the 15,000-square-foot building—also the institute’s headquarters—stocks goods inspired by the archives, including books and exhibition catalogs, miniature replicas of iconic furniture pieces, and toy tops reminiscent of those procured by the Eameses during their travels.

With an emphasis on the design process and creative problem-solving, the work encourages curiosity and learning. Demetrios observes that “when people can physically see the prototypes—all the different versions of something—it really has a faster impact on learning than just reading about it.”

Her grandparents’ exploration of materials has led Demetrios to rethink her own art. A third-generation bronze sculptor on her father’s side, when she resumes her practice, perhaps found objects will factor in. For now, though, Demetrios revels in sharing Charles and Ray’s story. “I’m sculpting experiences,” she says.
