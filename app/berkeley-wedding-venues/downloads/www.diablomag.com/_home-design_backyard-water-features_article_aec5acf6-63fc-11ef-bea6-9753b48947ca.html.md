In Woodside, Planted Earth Design and Build Co. collaborated with architecture firm Holder Parlette on a fountain and two waterfalls.

Samuel Henderson

As a second-generation pool builder, Diablo Valley native Chuck Baumann has witnessed firsthand the evolution of the industry. In the 1950s, his father started King Neptune Pools. By the time Baumann founded Alamo-based Creative Environments in 1979, he recognized that clients wanted more than just a swimming pool, hence the name he chose for his enterprise. “The pool designs of my dad’s era were one-dimensional,” he recalls. “They were flat—in the middle of the yard, with a whole bunch of concrete around them. Now they’ve become part of the landscaping, and there are all these wonderful visual treatments.”

Indeed, for many homeowners, outdoor areas have increasingly become personal oases. Water elements are commonly a focal point, with options available for practically any budget and backyard size. A 2021 report by the Freedonia Group forecasted that the demand for water features in the U.S. would bump up 1.1 percent per year to $1 billion in 2025, with the pandemic-driven attention on alfresco living a contributing factor.

## INTEGRATED WATERFALLS

Nearly every one of Baumann’s clients requests a water feature of some sort, which use recycled water from the pool. Creative Environments’ “average pool is north of $165,000,” he says, noting that a multitude of extras—like a waterfall, vanishing edge, or perimeter overflow—can push the price to over a quarter of a million dollars. In the 1990s, his business experienced a boom in waterfalls that were integrated into the landscaping. One or two of the 15 pools he builds annually still include a waterfall; these are generally pools with a natural look, achieved with rocks and boulders. Today’s architectural pools, Baumann says, are more compatible with sheer descents, which drop clean arcs of water.

![DM2409_054_DIG.jpg](data:image/png;base64,iVBORw0KGgoAAAANSUhEUgAAAAQAAAADCAQAAAAe/WZNAAAAEElEQVR42mM8U88ABowYDABAxQPltt5zqAAAAABJRU5ErkJggg==)

Creative Environments, led by project manager Nicco Baumann, completed an Alamo project with landscape design by Peter Koenig.

Jimi Smith Photography

According to local landscape architect and general contractor Michael McFadden, “a naturalistic waterfall is one of the hardest things to do. In nature, they happen because there’s water flow. Trying to recreate that with a liner underneath is quite tricky.” McFadden, who established Planted Earth Design and Build Co. in Berkeley in 2003 and has devised an array of water features since, has been known to take off his shoes, get in the water, and manually adjust stones to optimize the flow and sound. He pairs waterfalls with plants that evoke natural settings: for example, rushes, which typically grow in or near water.

For a recent project in Woodside, he worked with architect Chris Parlette of Marin County’s Holder Parlette on two recirculating waterfalls and a fountain. “My team and I purchased a crazy chainsaw that you can cut boulders with,” McFadden says, adding that Parlette’s renderings brought to mind artist Andy Goldsworthy, whose practice employs natural materials. McFadden and Parlette’s collaboration, comprising autumn creek stone—the large boulders that McFadden split along with smaller pieces contained in Gabion cages—is at once organic and modern, with a sculptural quality.

Planted Earth's bespoke water feature for an El Cerrito client offers sound and movement.

## SITE-SPECIFIC FOUNTAINS

While he designs custom fountains all over the Bay Area, McFadden often suggests simple fountains to his clients. “I call them ‘plug and play’—prefabricated water features that are easy to shop for,” he elaborates. “That’s something you can price very accurately from the get-go.” A stone or ceramic base with a small pump can cost a few hundred dollars and requires little maintenance.

In her own backyard, Clementine Jang—who cofounded Oakland landscape architecture firm Soft Studio with Lafayette native Jessie Booth—installed a custom fountain composed of a curved copper pipe and a natural boulder with a carved basin. The drainage basin is covered in gravel and a pump recirculates the water. With metalwork and stonemasonry involved, the cost was a few thousand dollars. The fountain is surrounded by seep monkey flower and yerba mansa, both California natives that love moisture. “Some of the water blows in the wind, bounces off the rock, and lands on the plants next to it,” Booth says of the thriving greenery.

![DM2409_061_DIG.jpg](data:image/png;base64,iVBORw0KGgoAAAANSUhEUgAAAAQAAAADCAQAAAAe/WZNAAAAEElEQVR42mM8U88ABowYDABAxQPltt5zqAAAAABJRU5ErkJggg==)

An Oakland garden by Soft Studio features a fountain with a basin carved by Yuren Estrada Santillan and pipe by Sendan Construction.

Clementine Jang

Soft Studio previously implemented the same concept for a client. “They live in Oakland over one of the culverted creeks, so reintroducing that sense of water to the site was nice,” Booth says. A camera allows the homeowners to watch birds engage with the fountain. A variation of the design is in the works for a garden in Orinda. In keeping with the architectural style of that residence, the fountain will incorporate an antique limestone trough.

## SERENE KOI PONDS

For homeowners like Wei Yao, who wanted to “bring relaxation, a view, and life to the landscape,” a koi pond was the right choice. He enlisted Noah Twitchell of Richmond-based Bay Area Koi Ponds for his Saratoga dwelling’s modern koi pond with a bridge over it. “You’re basically building an aesthetically pleasing life support system for the fish,” says Twitchell, emphasizing the importance of a properly constructed pond, as well as understanding the time and money it can entail.

![DM2409_060_DIG.jpg](data:image/png;base64,iVBORw0KGgoAAAANSUhEUgAAAAQAAAADCAQAAAAe/WZNAAAAEElEQVR42mM8U88ABowYDABAxQPltt5zqAAAAABJRU5ErkJggg==)

A Bay Area Koi Ponds project in Saratoga.

Noah Twitchell

In addition to designing and installing koi ponds, his firm offers remodeling and maintenance services. Even clients who take advantage of the latter usually partake in daily activities such as feeding the fish and emptying the skimmer that removes surface debris. To date, the largest pond that Twitchell conceived is 20,000 gallons, for Japanese-inspired grounds in Woodside, complete with a torii gate adjoining a water lily garden. The minimum size that Twitchell recommends is 2,000 gallons, which can accommodate a handful of koi, and, at a basic level, can cost about $35,000. For all the bells and whistles—like a waterfall or stream, boulders and rock work, underwater lighting, waterproofing, and an automated filter system—expect to pay closer to $50,000.

While Twitchell estimates that “a good starter koi” runs around $800, some clients have paid 10 times that amount for a fish, with size and age influencing the price. “Just like with shoes, there are name brands,” he continues. “There are breeders who have a really high reputation and who have won koi shows.” Twitchell also gets requests for goldfish, catfish, and turtles. He designs to thwart predators such as birds and raccoons: His ponds are at least four feet deep with vertical walls, plus shade sails, trees, and trellises overhead.

As visually appealing as fountains, waterfalls, and ponds are, the benefits of aquascaping can extend beyond aesthetics. Studies, like the one published in the _Journal of Environmental Psychology_ in 2022, have shown that looking at water decreases blood pressure and heart rate, while increasing relaxation. “People take a lot of time to drive to places like the Lake Tahoe area, where you get to a stream that’s feeding snowmelt into the lake,” observes Baumann, whose son, Nicco, recently joined him at Creative Environments. “You sit there and relax and meditate. If you have a water feature, you can just open your back door.”
