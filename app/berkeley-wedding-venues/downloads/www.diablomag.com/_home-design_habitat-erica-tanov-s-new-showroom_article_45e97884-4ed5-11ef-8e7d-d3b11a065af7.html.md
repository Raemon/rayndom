![DM2407_163_DIG.jpg](data:image/png;base64,iVBORw0KGgoAAAANSUhEUgAAAAQAAAADCAQAAAAe/WZNAAAAEElEQVR42mM8U88ABowYDABAxQPltt5zqAAAAABJRU5ErkJggg==)

The Erica Tanov showroom features pillows in her Fern and Super 1965 prints.

Molly Haas

Over the years, East Bay native Erica Tanov had passed the 1878 Italianate Victorian in Berkeley countless times. Located near her eponymous boutique on Fourth Street, it was a building that the fashion and home goods designer “had long admired and coveted,” she says. And, it turns out, “it checked all the boxes—plus more—on my list when I was looking for a space.”

In April, Tanov debuted a showroom in the three-story venue, which also houses her design studio. “I wanted to create an inviting and nontraditional space for interior designers and design enthusiasts to come peruse the breadth of our offerings, in a way I hadn’t experienced before,” she explains, noting that the by-appointment studio/showroom also aims to “provide an inspiring and relaxing setting for our design consultation services.”

![DM2407_203_DIG.jpg](data:image/png;base64,iVBORw0KGgoAAAANSUhEUgAAAAQAAAADCAQAAAAe/WZNAAAAEElEQVR42mM8U88ABowYDABAxQPltt5zqAAAAABJRU5ErkJggg==)

Erica Tanov

Alanna Hale

Beyond its arched double doors, the entrance level contains a parlor, kitchen, dining nook, bedroom, dressing room, and bathroom—arrayed with items, in context, that are available for purchase. Tanov’s signature home collection is joined by artwork, along with vintage and antique discoveries, from furniture to lighting to objets d’art. Revolving exhibitions often featuring the work of Bay Area artists and an event series called Et Al. also take place here.

After graduating from Parsons School of Design, Tanov launched her womens wear label in New York in 1990. Four years later, she returned to the Bay Area and opened her first store in a Berkeley building where her grandfather once operated his laundry business. “I realized my love and penchant for designing spaces in which I could create a complete experience,” she recalls of her foray into brick-and-mortar. “My collection then expanded to include home goods, initially bedding, using the same fabrics and bespoke prints that I used for clothing, then organically adding to the scope of offerings as ideas and different opportunities came about.”

![DM2407_164_DIG.jpg](data:image/png;base64,iVBORw0KGgoAAAANSUhEUgAAAAQAAAADCAQAAAAe/WZNAAAAEElEQVR42mM8U88ABowYDABAxQPltt5zqAAAAABJRU5ErkJggg==)

The 1965 wallpaper is named for Tanov’s birth year.

Molly Haas

Her bedding, decorative pillows, floor cushions, table linens, and fabrics by the yard populate the new showroom. Tiles from a collaboration with Clé are on display, while several rooms feature her hand-silkscreened wallpapers. She recently introduced the Yves furniture collection, which combines a modern aesthetic with heritage artisan techniques. This summer brings a capsule collection with San Francisco–based art and design firm Studio Ahead, comprising embroidered linen pieces.

“I have a passion for mixing pattern and texture, so most of my interior design products are meant to be interwoven with one another, layer upon layer, to create rich, tactile, inviting, and timeless environments,” Tanov explains. “Each piece is designed with intention and crafted with integrity.”
