Children’s Fairyland has been a beloved destination for families since 1950.

Children’s Fairyland | Jacqui June Whitlock

In 1950, few amusement parks were geared toward young kids—but Children’s Fairyland made its debut in Oakland that fall and changed everything. Over the last 75 years, the park has offered families a screen-free way to explore storybook worlds in a whimsical, three-dimensional setting.

“Parks just like it [later] sprang up all around, whether it’s Santa’s Wonderland, Enchanted Forest, or Fairytale Town in Sacramento, and it is one of the few that remains. It also [helped] inspire Walt Disney to make Disneyland,” says Kymberly Miller, Children’s Fairyland executive director. “It’s a unique, immersive experience that inspires the imagination for children and also allows parents to feel like this is a safe, comfortable, creative, informal learning space.”

For its anniversary, Fairyland collaborated on a retrospective exhibition with its partners at the Oakland Museum of California (OMCA). The result, _Fairyland @ 75: A Legacy of Magic,_ will run through December 7 at OMCA and features park miniatures and historical photos, as well as one of the original Jolly Trolly cars from the park. Fairyland is also hosting special programming this summer, culminating in a birthday party on September 1. The Fairyland team is particularly excited about their first English-Spanish bilingual puppet show and the latest installment of their 21+ series, Fairyland for Grownups, held with Oaklandish; both will take place in August.

Despite the decades, Miller says the original goal of Children’s Fairyland has remained a top priority. “We’re really about serving these little people in our community … giving kids the ability to use their imaginations to create their own universe based on stories that surround them from different cultures and places,” she says. _[fairyland.org](http://fairyland.org), [museumca.org](http://museumca.org)._
