**While California is renowned for its glorious, 840-mile-long coastline, it also has a unique collection of islands that offer seaside activities, exquisite natural beauty, and glimpses into our history. We rounded up the Golden State’s most fascinating islands and discovered that all of them—even the quirkiest islets—have unusual stories to tell. Read on to learn about these one-of-a-kind locales.**

## Offshore Islands

**Popular with sailors, divers, kayakers, hikers, and nature buffs, California’s most-visited islands never disappoint.**

The eight-island archipelago known as the **Channel Islands** sits just off the Central Coast regions of Santa Barbara and Ventura. They have a rich ecological diversity, with 145 endemic species finding a home here. Archaeological evidence has indicated that the oldest known human remains in the Americas may have been found in these parts. Certain Chumash Indian tribe members can trace their lineage back to these islands, and some say that the origin story of their people began in this region.

Five of the islands make up the protected Channel Islands National Park, which was established in 1980. Visitors, who arrive via private boat or by Island Packers, the park concessionaire boat company that operates from Ventura and Oxnard harbors, will find myriad scenic hiking trails. The trip is anywhere from one to four-and-a-half hours each way depending on your destination; choose among Santa Rosa, San Miguel, Santa Cruz, and Anacapa islands. There are campsites on these islands as well, which provide a memorable way to appreciate the environment.

![DM2412_064_DIG.jpg](data:image/png;base64,iVBORw0KGgoAAAANSUhEUgAAAAQAAAADCAQAAAAe/WZNAAAAEElEQVR42mM8U88ABowYDABAxQPltt5zqAAAAABJRU5ErkJggg==)

Whale-watching opportunities off the Channel Islands peak during the winter months.

Bob Perry

For an even deeper dive into the Channel Islands, the National Geographic-Lindblad Expeditions tour is an extraordinarily immersive trip, featuring a team of expert naturalists and offering experiences on the islands and in the aquatic environs that you simply can’t get on your own. Some of those naturalists have lived and worked on the islands, and they can provide rare insights into the islands’ history and habitat.

The surrounding waters are part of the Channel Islands National Marine Sanctuary (CINMS), designated by the National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration in 1980 to protect the sea life and ecosystems in this 1,470-square-mile area encompassing the islands in the Channel Islands National Park. CINMS and Channel Islands National Park lead the Naturalist Corps program, a network of trained volunteers dedicated to educating the public about the Channel Islands. The volunteers lead hikes and accompany whale watchers, reaching thousands of people a year through their outreach.

![apeacock_usa_191005-41652, USA230.jpg](data:image/png;base64,iVBORw0KGgoAAAANSUhEUgAAAAQAAAADCAQAAAAe/WZNAAAAEElEQVR42mM8U88ABowYDABAxQPltt5zqAAAAABJRU5ErkJggg==)

Catalina was also used as the location for a variety of early Hollywood films, including _The Vanishing American_ (1925) and _The Thundering Herd_ (1933), for which more than a dozen American bison were transported to the island. ## Urban Islands

**Unlike offshore islands that often transport visitors into the wild, some of California’s islands are right smack in the middle of things, so close to urban hubs they almost feel like part of the mainland.**

At the top of the state is **Woodley Island,** located in Humboldt Bay across from Old Town Eureka. It has a large marina for both recreational and commercial boats as well as the historic Table Bluff Lighthouse.

![23859715](data:image/png;base64,iVBORw0KGgoAAAANSUhEUgAAAAQAAAADCAQAAAAe/WZNAAAAEElEQVR42mM8U88ABowYDABAxQPltt5zqAAAAABJRU5ErkJggg==)

The historic USS _Hornet_ aircraft carrier draws thousands of visitors to Alameda every year.

Shutterstock

In the Bay Area, **Alameda** is an island of some 80,000 residents, separated from neighboring Oakland by an estuary. At just over 10 square miles, it is easy to forget that Alameda is even an island, but the seaside views at Alameda Point—home to a popular monthly antiques fair and the burgeoning Spirits Alley area, housing craft distilleries and tasting rooms—will remind you that this is indeed an island zone. A bit farther north along the East Bay is **Mare Island** , which is actually a peninsula across from Vallejo in the middle of San Pablo Bay. The site of the first naval station on the West Coast, Mare Island can be explored via guided tour offered by the Mare Island Historic Park Foundation.

![1075392455](data:image/png;base64,iVBORw0KGgoAAAANSUhEUgAAAAQAAAADCAQAAAAe/WZNAAAAEElEQVR42mM8U88ABowYDABAxQPltt5zqAAAAABJRU5ErkJggg==)

The Fun Zone is a popular amusement park on Balboa Island.

Shutterstock

In the middle of the bay are two connected islands, easily accessed by the San Francisco-Oakland Bay Bridge. **Treasure Island** is worth a visit for its regular TreasureFest, and neighboring **Yerba Buena,** a natural island, is home to the 69-foot-tall _Point of Infinity_ sundial by Hiroshi Sugimoto, which was erected in 2023.

![211243189](data:image/png;base64,iVBORw0KGgoAAAANSUhEUgAAAAQAAAADCAQAAAAe/WZNAAAAEElEQVR42mM8U88ABowYDABAxQPltt5zqAAAAABJRU5ErkJggg==)

The Lost Fisherman’s Memorial, built in 1981, is an iconic statue on Woodley Island.

Shutterstock

At the southern stretch of the state is **Coronado** , tucked into the curve of San Diego’s hip, with a historic hotel and killer beaches. Nearby **Fiesta Island,** a peninsula in San Diego’s Mission Bay, is a large piece of land popular for all sorts of recreation. To the north, **Balboa Island,** off the coast of Newport Beach, is a man-made island that offers a range of activities. A bit farther up the coast, in Long Beach, you’ll come upon **Naples** (which is actually three islands separated by canals), a destination with a heavy Italian theme and a robust boating community.

## Offbeat Islands

**Tiny, unexpected, and intriguing, these islands have compelling profiles.**

**Alcatraz,** a 22.5-acre island in San Francisco Bay, needs little introduction. The maximum-security federal penitentiary, now a museum, operated just shy of 30 years before closing in the early 1960s. Between 1969 and 1971, the Indians of All Tribes occupied the island for 19 months as an activist statement for Native American civil rights.

Dubbed the “Ellis Island of the West,” **Angel Island,** which is just a short ferry ride from Tiburon, was the processing site for hundreds of thousands of immigrants from 1910 to 1940. Today, you can hike around the entire island—which is administered by the California State Parks system—or take a tram tour that explains its history. Don’t miss the Immigration Station, where you can learn more about the injustices inflicted on Asian immigrants during World War II.

Tiny **East Brother Island,** located off Point San Pablo in Richmond, is best known for East Brother Light Station, a grouping of three historic buildings, the main one being a lighthouse constructed in 1873 that now operates as a bed-and-breakfast.

Thirty miles west of the Golden Gate Bridge are the rocky **Farallon Islands,** which have been designated a National Wildlife Refuge intended to protect seabirds and marine mammals.

**Rat Rock Island,** an 18-foot-tall speck of place, is situated just off China Camp State Park in San Rafael and offers a nice photo opportunity for park-goers.

The **Sacramento–San Joaquin Delta** —which spans several counties (including Contra Costa, Sacramento, San Joaquin, Solano, and Yolo)—consists of some 200 islands surrounded by waterways and levees. A popular destination for fishing and all kinds of water sports, it’s also home to white sturgeon prized for their caviar.
