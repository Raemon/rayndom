Justin Katigbak

The city of **Portland** is delightfully weird. Home to what’s said to be the world’s largest independent bookstore, a huge urban forest, and a food scene exploding with independent coffee roasters, hundreds of foods carts, and every cuisine imaginable, the city is a perfect summer destination. And nonstop less-than-two-hour flights from San Francisco Bay Oakland International Airport make it an easy trip.

Understanding and appreciating Portland is all about getting to know its quirky walkable neighborhoods; grasping some basics can go a long way toward getting oriented. Split east and west by the Willamette River and north and south by Burnside Street, the city has more than 90 distinct neighborhoods. It is also easy to get to outdoor adventures. One weekend won’t even start to cover Portland’s diverse attractions, but it will give you a good sampling and a strong desire to return.

### Stroll and Shop

After landing at Portland International Airport, many visitors head straight for downtown. Although the area is still feeling the effects of the pandemic, there is an eclectic mix of shops, eateries, and hotels here, along with colorful neighborhoods in the environs. Put on a good pair of walking shoes and wander. The **Pearl District** is home to myriad art galleries and one of the world’s most famous bookstores, **Powell’s City of Books.** Occupying an entire city block, the new and used bookstore features approximately one million volumes; go to the third floor to snag a pass to the Rare Book Room, chock-full of collectible reads and assorted autographed offerings.

Across the street is **MadeHere,** which showcases products crafted in Portland and the Pacific Northwest. From teas and jewelry to ceramics, wooden bowls, leather goods, and clothing, you’ll find works from 150 different makers under one roof. Walk a few blocks back to downtown, and you can shop for Japanese kitchenware at **Kokoro,** Japanese textiles at sister boutique **Kiriko Made,** or colorful gifts and home goods at **Woonwinkel.** Oregon has no sales tax, so shopping budgets go a bit further.

Head to Portland for the Japanese-Korean fusion eats at Janken.

Thomas Teal

Dining options are just as varied. For a caffeine fix, try local favorites **Stumptown Coffee Roasters** and **Courier Coffee,** or enjoy hot and cold single-origin chocolate beverages at **Roste Chocolate House.** Folks line up outside the bright pink **Voodoo Doughnut** facade to get their hands on colorful confections topped with everything from bacon and bubble gum to nostalgic breakfast cereals. Stop for dinner at **Janken,** which has mouthwatering Japanese-Korean fusion dishes like japchae and miso black cod, alongside creative cocktails.

**Bellpine,** located on the 20th floor of the **Ritz-Carlton, Portland,** serves breakfast, lunch, and dinner, complemented by a bird’s-eye view of the city. A newcomer to downtown, the Ritz-Carlton features 251 rooms, a spa, and a sparkling indoor pool where guests can splash in the glow of the city skyline. Built in 1927, the **Heathman Hotel** offers 151 rooms and an enchanting library of more than 3,000 books, mostly first editions. Many of the books can be checked out and taken to your room.

Situated on the main drag of the **Alberta Arts District,** the soon-to-open **Cascada** hotel boasts Portland’s first underground hot springs and spa. Following a therapeutic soak, head to **Mister OK’s Essentials** to create your own candle, shop for botanical wellness products at **Blendily,** or spoil a little one with something fun from **Grasshopper** toy and clothing store. The city’s legendary food cart scene promises something for every taste; at **Alberta Carts,** standouts include wood-fired pizza at **Paladin Pie,** barbecue pork brisket tacos at **Wonderboy’s Smokestack,** and lumpia and ube rice cake at **Baon Kainan.**

### Open-Air Adventures

With more than 200 parks and gardens within city limits, a love for the great outdoors is woven into Portland’s ethos. Set atop a cinder cone, **Mount Tabor Park** offers views of downtown Portland, Mount Hood, and Mount St. Helens on a clear day. A set of trail guides put together by the nonprofit Friends of Mt. Tabor Park makes it easy for newcomers to explore. From the park, it’s less than a 10-minute drive to the Richmond neighborhood, where you can’t go wrong with roti _canai_ (a flaky flatbread served with parsnip and squash curry) at **Oma’s Hideaway** or a slice of pie from **Lauretta Jean’s Pie Bakery**. Tasty options run the gamut from passion fruit and banana cream to key lime and chocolate peanut butter.

Stretching 5,200 acres, **Forest Park** features 80-plus miles of trails lined with towering hemlocks, Douglas firs, hazelnut trees, and much more. **Around Portland Tours** guides morning hikes that cross into **Washington Park,** where you can catch a whiff of the more than 10,000 rose bushes planted at the **International Rose Test Garden.** (Depending on the weather, roses bloom from late May through October.) If time and energy levels allow, the tranquil **Portland Japanese Garden** is just steps away.

### Raise a Glass in the Willamette Valley

An hour or so south of Portland, the Willamette Valley wine region is synonymous with pinot noir, but chardonnay drinkers will find something to love here too. **Soter Vineyards** is part of **Mineral Springs Ranch,** a 240-acre biodynamic farm complete with chickens, cows, sheep, pigs, turkeys, goats, and vineyards. (If the Soter name sounds familiar, it’s because Tony Soter left a lasting mark in Napa Valley, working with wineries like Chappellet, Spottswoode, and Shafer, as well as creating Etude, before making the move north.)

The Mineral Springs Provisions Tasting experience pairs a flight of sparkling and still wines with dishes that change depending on the ranch’s seasonal bounty; miso soup, English peas and wild onions, braised rabbit, and rhubarb crisp with magnolia ice cream are just some of the possibilities.

**Artist Block** is a must-visit stop for fans of modern art who are looking for a more relaxed wine tasting opportunity. Pop art–inspired labels include a rosé named Rainbow Juice and a sparkling gamay noir known as Cha-Ching Cha-Ching. Charcuterie and cheese boards can include goldfish crackers and gummy bears. Just add the unusual tasting options to your list of quintessential Portland experiences.
