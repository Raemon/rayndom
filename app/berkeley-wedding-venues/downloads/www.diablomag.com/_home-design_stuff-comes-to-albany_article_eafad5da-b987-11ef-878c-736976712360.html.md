![DM2412_047_DIG.jpg](data:image/png;base64,iVBORw0KGgoAAAANSUhEUgAAAAQAAAADCAQAAAAe/WZNAAAAEElEQVR42mM8U88ABowYDABAxQPltt5zqAAAAABJRU5ErkJggg==)

The wares at Stuff Vintage Modern include art, furniture, and retro collectibles.

Courtesy of Stuff Vintage Modern

For 13 years, Stuff was a bright orange beacon for vintage modern goods in San Francisco’s Mission District. An Instagram post last year announcing its closure was met with a slew of heartbreak and crying emojis. The emporium featured more than 60 vendors—including Angelina and Joe Carling. When the doors shut in February 2024, the couple had been selling at Stuff for five years. “We wanted to keep it going,” Joe says. “And since we live in the East Bay, we wanted to open here.”

The next month—with Stuff owners Will Lenker and James Spinello granting them permission to use the same moniker—the Carlings debuted their incarnation in Albany. The 9,000-square-foot venue channels the defunct San Francisco collective’s upbeat vibe, from the orange facade to the red carpeting to the ’80s-heavy playlist.

![DM2412_048_DIG.jpg](data:image/png;base64,iVBORw0KGgoAAAANSUhEUgAAAAQAAAADCAQAAAAe/WZNAAAAEElEQVR42mM8U88ABowYDABAxQPltt5zqAAAAABJRU5ErkJggg==)

Courtesy of Stuff Vintage Modern

The purveyors and range of wares feel familiar, too. Joe estimates that about a dozen Stuff sellers crossed the bay to his San Pablo Avenue outpost, Stuff Vintage Modern—combining with new vendors for a total of about 30 to date. While clothing comprises roughly 20 percent of the inventory, shoppers primarily encounter myriad styles of furniture, accessories, artwork, and knick-knacks. He points to a porcelain figurine that dates to the 1800s and a selection of Persian rugs to illustrate the venue’s diverse offerings.

Still, the midcentury designs are a big draw, with pieces by the likes of Heywood- Wakefield, Arthur Umanoff, and Charles and Ray Eames. “Basically, we carry the kinds of things that stores are trying to duplicate nowadays,” Joe says. “They’re trying to copy the stuff that we have the originals of—which are much better quality. A lot of the new stuff, if you move it from one house to another, it might fall apart in your hands. What we have is original and built very well, very precise.”

![DM2412_049_DIG.jpg](data:image/png;base64,iVBORw0KGgoAAAANSUhEUgAAAAQAAAADCAQAAAAe/WZNAAAAEElEQVR42mM8U88ABowYDABAxQPltt5zqAAAAABJRU5ErkJggg==)

Courtesy of Stuff Vintage Modern

For the past decade, Joe and his wife have been collecting vintage items—scouring estate sales and auctions. With their adult children also pitching in, Stuff Vintage Modern is a family affair. Joe restores the furniture as well, tapping into skills developed during his 30-or-so years as “a body and paint guy on cars,” as he puts it. At the start of 2024, Joe and Angelina, who was a secretary, both retired to focus on the shop.

“We’re putting our all into this business,” he says, noting that the couple works side by side daily. “We love finding things that we know other people are going to love. The name Stuff says a lot: There’s something for everybody.” _[stuffvintagemodern.com](http://stuffvintagemodern.com)._
