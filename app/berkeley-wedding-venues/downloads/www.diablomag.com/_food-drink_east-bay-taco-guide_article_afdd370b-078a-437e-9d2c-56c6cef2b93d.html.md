**Who doesn’t love a taco? Indeed, it seems as if these tortilla-backed canvases for deliciousness have become the East Bay’s de facto official food—and now there are more variations than ever before. Want California-inflected versions highlighting seasonal produce? Spit-roasted al pastor at a street stand washed down with a can of Modelo? Modern spins on Mexican American classics backed by a cocktail made with 100 percent agave tequila? How about an heirloom corn tortilla tostada bursting with inventive flavors or a plate of crispy-shelled go-tos favored by even the pickiest of taco fans? Our region offers all of those options and so much more as the local taco scene continues to innovate, evolve, and, most of all, satisfy. Because as Lito Saldaña of 5 Tacos and Beers puts it: “You just can’t go wrong with a taco.”**

** _Diablo_ spotlights five mouthwatering examples of pork, seafood, beef, chicken, and veggie tacos available in the East Bay, along with dozens of additional choices across each filling category. Enjoy!**

## Recipe for Excellence

**Carnitas tacos / 5 Tacos and Beers / Albany, Berkeley, and Walnut Creek**

➳ Chef Lito Saldaña combined the popularity of Taco Tuesdays at his traditional Emeryville restaurant Los Moles with a family trip through beer gardens in Germany and hatched the idea for 5 Tacos and Beers. The restaurant’s modern, relaxed setting paired with homemade tortillas, quality meats, and craft beer proved an instant hit—and led to three new 5 Tacos and Beers locations in the last three years. “It’s not a taqueria, but it’s not fine dining,” he explains. “It’s a place with nice ambience that’s still casual, where you can feel comfortable and have fun.” The carnitas tacos are a bestseller and a personal favorite of Saldaña, who uses the family recipe he grew up eating in a small town in Jalisco. All-natural pork is seared in hot oil in a stainless steel _cazo_ pot before it is slowly simmered with oranges, onions, garlic, and salt for four hours. The tender result is then placed on a tortilla and topped with a curtido slaw of jalapeños, red onions, tomatoes, lime, and cilantro. The specific cut of pork is rotated monthly while the cooking method stays the same. “That’s the way we grew up having it: My dad would wake up early in the morning to get the meat from the market, [and] my mom would make her own tortillas,” Saldaña says. “They are meant to be homestyle tacos.”

### On the Pork Taco Trail

****> > **Regional: ****Tacos Livermore** specializes in the flavors of Jalisco, including spit-roasted pork al pastor tacos topped with sautéed onions, salsa, and sweet hints of pineapple.

****> > **Upscale: **Walnut Creek hot spot **Lita** puts a Caribbean-Latin spin on its tacos with coconut-braised pork belly with roasted pineapple and citrus crema atop blue corn tortillas.

****> > **Classic comfort: **For simple, satisfying street tacos, **Tortilleria El Molino** ’s carnitas (and al pastor and carne asada) have been a go-to for decades; the Concord institution also makes its own widely sold corn tortillas.

****> > **California twist: **Fine-dining-trained chef Daniel Patlan employs farm-to-table touches at Pleasant Hill’s **El Aguila,** which includes tacos with scratch-made chorizo.

****> > **Trend watch: **Authentic al pastor. Legit spit-fired al pastor tacos can be hard to find in the East Bay. **Tacos El Charro** is making the search easier as the viral sensation’s pineapple-topped _trompos_ stacked with dripping adobo-marinated pork can be ordered at streetside food stands across the East Bay, including in Berkeley, Livermore, San Leandro, and Union City.

## Tastes of the Sea

**Trout tostadas / Bombera / Oakland**

➳ In coming up with her trout tostadas, Bombera chef-owner Dominica Rice-Cisneros connected her fondness for deli-style smoked fish with memories of childhood vacations to seafood-rich Baja, Mexico. “I really liked the idea of putting those two things together,” she says. Rice-Cisneros complements her smoked trout sourced from Monterey Fish Market with sun gold cherry tomatoes and fried shallots, arranges everything on a crispy corn tortilla—made in-house from Oaxacan maize masa—and then smears it with cool avocado crema. That, in turn, is perched on a bed of arugula dressed with mustard vinaigrette and pickled jalapeños. If the dish sounds like a high-concept entrée you might see at Chez Panisse, that’s because Rice-Cisneros worked at Alice Waters’s Berkeley institution, in addition to Michelin-starred restaurants in New York and Mexico City. Rice-Cisneros returned to her Mexican roots at the artisan taco destination Cosecha 15 years ago, then moved on to Bombera in 2022; there she explores more elevated expressions of lesser-known Mexican fare but still finds a place on the menu for tacos made with her homemade tortillas. “For me, tortillas are everything—they were my utensil growing up as a kid,” she says. “They’re a kind of birthright that goes back thousands of years into my heritage.”

### On the Seafood Taco Trail

****> > **Classic comfort:** Known for its simple, satisfying grilled shrimp and fish street tacos, as well as ceviche tostadas, **Tacos Sinaloa** has been a fixture in East Oakland for 25-plus years; it now operates a brick-and-mortar on Telegraph Avenue near the UC Berkeley campus.

****> > **Regional:** There may be no more beloved Baja import than batter-fried fish tacos with crema and slaw. **Cholita Linda** ’s impeccable renditions have helped fuel its expansion from Oakland to Alameda, Berkeley, San Francisco, and Walnut Creek.

**> > Modern family:** Fun, colorful **Bar Camino** in Walnut Creek puts a spicy, garlicky spin on shrimp tacos with its hard-shelled camarones dorados. Wash it down with a Horchatalada slushy.

**> > California twist:** Ahi poke crispy tacos with sushi-grade marinated fish, avocado, and spicy mayo are a standout burger alternative at **Gott’s** Walnut Creek location.

**> > Trend watch:** Hard-shelled tacos. Cafeteria-style hard-shelled tacos are back in a big way, and there may be no more ostentatious example than the five-pound “No Mames” taco served in a U-shaped fried flour tortilla at Dublin’s **Taqueria Azteca.**

## Flavors from Home

**Barbacoa tacos / Las Guerreras Comida Costeña / Oakland**

➳ Reyna Maldonado’s mom Ofelia sold tamales for 15 years in San Francisco’s Mission District, but when the mother-daughter duo opened their first brick-and-mortar restaurant in Oakland six years ago, they got one question more than any other: Where’s your barbacoa? Ofelia’s version of the slow-cooked tender beef is aggressively rubbed with pasilla, guajillo, and ancho chiles and cooked for up to four hours in banana leaves to lend it a layered earthy, spicy aroma and flavor unique to her home state of Guerrero, Mexico. “My mom brought that recipe here with us,” says Reyna. “It’s part of our migration story.” Earlier this year, the Maldonados leaned in even further to their heritage by expanding and rebranding their restaurant in Oakland’s Swan Market to Las Guerreras Comida Costeña. They now feature seafood dishes popular in the coastal Mexican region and even added a stand-alone ceviche bar—although barbacoa remains the top seller. “It’s really important to highlight where we’re from,” says Reyna. “Guerrero is something special and sacred to us.”

### On the Beef Taco Trail

**> > Classic comfort:** Tender _suadero_ is a favorite at **Taqueria El Paisa@ .com** , arguably the most in-demand among the many purveyors of authentic street tacos along Oakland’s International Boulevard.

**> > Upscale: Posada Alta Cocina**’s popular citrus- and chili-marinated rib eye is sliced into tacos for lunch service at this longtime Latin fine-dining destination in Livermore.

**> > Modern family:** Lively Mexican American throwback **Cantina Jack’s** in Pleasant Hill offers “tacos Americana” with seasoned ground beef topped with shredded lettuce, tomato, and cheddar in an old-school hard shell.

**> > California twist: Gold Star Tacos** makes everything from scratch for its periodic Tex-Mex breakfast taco pop-up at Oakland’s Sfizio restaurant, including slow-cooked carne _guisada_ (stewed beef) inside fresh-griddled flour tortillas.

**> > Trend watch:** _Quesabirria._ These gooey quesadilla-tacos featuring saucy stewed beef griddled with cheese and served with beef consommé dipping sauce are huge in the East Bay. Pleasant Hill’s **Tacos El Patron** has been serving them up with panache since before they were trendy.

## Pollo Perfection

**Crispy chicken tacos / Los Panchos / Danville and Pleasant Hill**

➳ Los Panchos has been a Contra Costa County mainstay for years, but the family restaurant’s popular crispy chicken tacos involve a special alchemy that might be surprising for a seemingly simple dish. 

“Getting everything tasting [great] with the right consistency and texture takes a little bit of magic,” says Fernando Fernandez, who co-owns the two Los Panchos locations with his three sisters.

The taco filling starts with a whole chicken that is first boiled, deconstructed, and then stewed with onions, tomatoes, and a secret mix of spices and seasonings to optimize flavor. The meat goes into a fresh tortilla which is clamped and deep fried as a whole. “We individually deep fry every taco,” says Fernandez. “It’s not as simple as just grabbing meat and stuffing it into a shell.” The taco is then cooled slightly, unclamped, and topped with lettuce and Monterey cheese for added freshness and creaminess.

In addition to the chicken tacos, other top dishes at Los Panchos include burritos, enchiladas, and chile rellenos.

Fernandez’s parents, Gloria and Aurelio, opened their first restaurant in Pacheco in 1991, drawing on family recipes and inspiration from Gloria’s time working at other local eateries. The contemporary menu stays true to those original recipes, and Fernandez says that consistency, quality ingredients, and customer service are what keep diners coming back year after year.

“We really enjoy being part of the community and getting to know our customers,” adds Fernandez. “[Our family] is very hands-on; even my father, who is in his 80s, comes by once in a while to make our pastor sauce. He is the only one who makes it and is keeping that recipe to himself for now.” **—Rachel Orvino**

### On the Chicken Taco Trail

**> > Regional:** Oaxacan-born chef Octavio Diaz uses more than 20 ingredients in his mole negro recipe, and **Agave Uptown** ’s signature mole chicken is served with rice, beans, and fresh-made tortillas to enjoy in taco form in Oakland.

**> > Upscale:** Danville’s elevated Mexican destination **Cielito Cocina Mexicana** fires up the adobo-marinated chicken in its al pastor tacos on a wood-flame grill and then serves them on homemade tortillas with roasted molcajete salsa.

**> > Modern family: Que Onda! Taco Bar,** with locations around the East Bay, puts a hip spin on its fast-casual eatery. Personalized dining options abound, including getting the pollo asado served _de costra_ with a layer of crispy, caramelized cheese griddled into a corn tortilla.

**> > California twist:** A surprise dusting of shaved Italian ricotta _salata_ covers the chipotle-chicken _tinga_ tostada, which can be enjoyed al fresco in the “margarita garden” at **Nido’s Backyard** in Oakland.

**> > Trend watch:** Charcoal-grilled meats. Northern Mexico–inspired tacos _al carbon_ highlight smoky meats cooked over a charcoal grill. Notable examples include those from food stands **La Parrilla Loca** in Oakland and **Tacos Mama Cuca** in Hayward.

## Veggie Tales

**Charred broccoli tacos / Tacos Oscar / Oakland**

➳ The origin story for Tacos Oscar’s most popular menu item isn’t quite as exciting as you might think. “I was barbecuing some broccoli I’d just got from the farmers market, forgot about it, and basically burnt the [crap] out of it,” remembers cofounder Oscar Michel. In an attempt to salvage things, he added a peanut salsa he’d been recipe-testing, and a legend was born. Indeed, charred broccoli with peanut-arbol salsa, cilantro, and pickled onions nestled on one of the restaurant’s signature fresh-griddled corn tortillas is one of the only constants on the weekly-changing menu at this cool, colorful taco destination set amid repurposed shipping containers in Oakland’s Temescal District. Michel and co-owner Jake Weiss have become well known for their vegetarian tacos that make creative use of seasonal produce—grilled corn and sugar snap peas with jalapeño vinaigrette, for example, or pan-fried sweet potatoes with queso fresco and salsa _macha_ —an approach that makes perfect sense in temperate, bountiful Northern California. Still, Michel admits they are lucky that broccoli is one vegetable available year-round. “People freak out if we take it off the menu,” he says.

### On the Vegetarian Taco Trail

**> > Upscale:** Organic wild mushrooms are topped with a heaping nest of crispy fried leeks and served on gorgeous deep blue heirloom corn tortillas crisped up with melted cheese at Pleasant Hill’s elegant **T’zunun.**

**> > Regional:** Sometimes referred to as the carnitas capital of the world, Michoacán is the culinary inspiration for Emeryville Public Market’s new vegan kiosk **Alma y Sazon,** which makes its version of carnitas with oyster mushrooms.

**> > Modern family: Puesto** serves Instagram-worthy dishes at Concord’s Veranda shopping center, including a small forest of creamy potato- and queso-fresco-filled fried taquitos that are plated upright in a bed of guacamole.

**> > Classic comfort:** Corn, zucchini, poblano chili, and pumpkin seed–cilantro salsa star in one of the many veggie-friendly options at **Tacubaya,** a beacon for California-inflected Mexican fare for two decades in Berkeley.

**> > Trend watch:** Vegan tacos. Rico Rico in Oakland made its name with veggie tacos, so much so that it opened **Rico Rico Vegano** in 2023. It’s been joined by **Taqueria La Venganza** (Oakland) and Alma y Sazon as full-time vegan taquerias in the East Bay.
