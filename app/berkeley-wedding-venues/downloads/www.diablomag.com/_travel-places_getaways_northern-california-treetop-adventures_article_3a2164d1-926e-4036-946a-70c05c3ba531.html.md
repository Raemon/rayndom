**It’s hard to put into words the feelings that may arise when you are surrounded by massive stands of trees. There’s something about leaving the everyday behind and exploring under the cool shade of these lofty giants that brings joy.**

**On their own, Northern California’s landmark redwoods make a compelling case to hop in the car and revel in these natural wonders, but t****here are other attractions, including brilliant seascapes and sky-high thrills, that round out such adventures. Read on for three drivable destinations that will leave you inspired, humbled, and breathing just a little easier.**

## Coastal Calm: Big Sur

Big Sur is a bit of a drama queen, at least when it comes to its strikingly rugged landscape. Just a few hours’ drive from the East Bay, it’s located on Highway 1, arguably one of the country’s most beautiful road trips. Flanked by the Santa Lucia mountains and the Pacific Ocean, there are many magical vistas, and not all of them are centered on the sea.

Set 1,200 feet above the Pacific on the cliffs of Big Sur, Post Ranch Inn has a sense of serenity woven into its architecture. Accommodations vary, but the freestanding, triangular Tree House rooms are among the most whimsical options. Elevated nine feet off the ground on stilts and accessible via stairs, these striking and unique dwellings are part of the property’s canopy. Walls wrapped in old-growth redwood repurposed from wine barrels help the luxurious shelters—featuring custom furnishings, fireplaces, and large windows—blend harmoniously into the scenery.

Forest meditation classes are offered regularly at Post Ranch Inn, but hiking trails through redwood groves and scenic meadows easily allow for solo moments of peace. That said, don’t be surprised if you happen upon deer and turkeys or spy a spouting whale as you savor the tranquility and occasional ocean views.

When visiting Big Sur, it can be a little unsettling to pass up the most popular stops, but the current closure of the McWay Falls trail at Julia Pfeiffer Burns State Park (repair work on an extensive retaining wall is slated to continue through 2026) provides the opportunity for new-to-you ventures. The Redwood Deck, an accessible wooden boardwalk at Pfeiffer Big Sur State Park, makes it simple to admire the impressive redwoods. For a breathtaking experience, consider the Buzzard’s Roost hike; the trail, which is three miles round trip, includes a stretch that climbs through stately redwoods. Located in the park, Big Sur Lodge’s restaurant is a favorite place to fuel up. Drawing from locally sourced ingredients, the diverse menus feature a variety of vegetarian and gluten-free options.

Swap redwoods for cypress trees at Point Lobos State Natural Reserve, about three miles south of Carmel-by-the-Sea. The Cypress Grove Trail curls through one of just two remaining naturally growing stands of Monterey cypress trees on the planet. The other is Crocker Grove, Stop 11 on the nearby 17-Mile Drive in Pebble Beach.

## Elevated Wine Country Experiences: Sonoma County

![DM2505_025_DIG.jpg](data:image/png;base64,iVBORw0KGgoAAAANSUhEUgAAAAQAAAADCAQAAAAe/WZNAAAAEElEQVR42mM8U88ABowYDABAxQPltt5zqAAAAABJRU5ErkJggg==)

Enjoy a flight through the forest followed by an overnight stay on high at Sonoma Zipline Adventures.

Courtesy of Sonoma Zipline Adventures

The river town of Guerneville was known as Stumptown during the mid-19th century, thanks to the redwood logging industry that called it home. Set aside for preservation by a wealthy lumberman in the late 1800s, Armstrong Redwoods State Natural Reserve now offers an awe-inspiring outing with trails suitable for all ages and fitness levels.

Among the park’s notable residents is the Colonel Armstrong Tree, the oldest tree in the grove, believed to be more than 1,400 years old. At over 310 feet (more than the length of a football field), the Parson Jones Tree is the tallest.

You could spend an entire day rambling through the 805-acre grove; however, for a close-up look at the world in the upper branches, make your way along the Bohemian Highway to Sonoma Zipline Adventures in nearby Occidental. Here, a pair of zip line courses allow thrill seekers to whiz along elevated speedways to an aerial village of tree houses, where they dine on sky-high room service before drifting off to sleep in the canopy of a redwood grove. The yurt-like dwellings feature a queen bed, set of bunk beds, sink, and composting toilet.

![DM2505_089_DIG.jpg](data:image/png;base64,iVBORw0KGgoAAAANSUhEUgAAAAQAAAADCAQAAAAe/WZNAAAAEElEQVR42mM8U88ABowYDABAxQPltt5zqAAAAABJRU5ErkJggg==)

Spyglass Treehouse offers a restorative retreat among the branches.

Courtesy of Spyglass Treehouse

If you want to admire Sonoma County from above but prefer a more easygoing experience, Eagle’s Nest Treehouse in Bodega Bay delivers a secluded stay on a working ranch. Slowly take the 30-foot spiral staircase up to the tree house and let the excitement build, cresting once you step onto a wraparound deck overlooking a 200-acre forested section of the ranch.

Amenities such as a queen bed, heat, electricity, a coffee machine, and a full bathroom promote a soothing overnight escape. Come morning, traverse trails that provide views of Salmon Creek and, on a clear day, the Pacific Ocean. Or get to know the long list of ranch residents, including Scottish Highland and Angus cattle, Kiko goats, ducks, and a half dozen or so working Anatolian shepherd guard dogs.

For all the comforts of a top-rated hotel room without the crowds, check out the Spyglass Treehouse in Occidental. Along with a king bed, floor-to-ceiling windows, and a bathroom featuring an infrared sauna, the Spyglass has a cedar hot tub ideal for soaking in timber views.

## Big Tree Territory: Humboldt County

Humboldt County is home to some of the tallest trees in the world, including those at the Redwood National and State Parks. Safeguarding nearly half of the world’s protected ancient redwood forest, the park complex is a World Heritage Site and an International Biosphere Reserve.

A four-hour-or-so drive from the East Bay, the Avenue of the Giants is a 30-plus-mile scenic introduction to the region. The route snakes through Humboldt Redwoods State Park, home to the largest stand of ancient redwoods left on the planet. Also known as State Route 254, the Highway 101 alternative was built in the 1880s as a thruway for wagons and stagecoaches.

There are multiple hikes where you can immerse yourself in the forest. Bolling Grove was the first grove to be purchased and preserved by Save the Redwoods League (it was dedicated in 1921); Founders Grove is home to the Dyerville Giant, believed by many to be the largest fallen redwood tree in the world.

You won’t find any tree houses in these parts, but the historic Scotia Lodge offers a quiet stay with a convenient location at the northern entrance of the Avenue of the Giants. Built in 1923 as the Mowatoc Hotel, the property once served as a stagecoach stop between the Bay Area and Eureka.

Trek among the treetops at Sequoia Park Zoo’s Redwood Sky Walk in Eureka. The longest skywalk in the Western United States, it features a suspended network of nine platforms and 10 bridges that stretch just under a quarter-mile through a grove of old-growth and second-growth redwoods. (There are no stairs, and much of the walk is ADA-accessible.)

Revel in a self-guided stroll, and keep looking up. At the highest point, you’ll be 100 feet in the air, while the upper canopy of the redwood trees soars approximately 250 feet. It’s a whole different world, and you’ll head home determined to expand your woodland explorations in the future.
