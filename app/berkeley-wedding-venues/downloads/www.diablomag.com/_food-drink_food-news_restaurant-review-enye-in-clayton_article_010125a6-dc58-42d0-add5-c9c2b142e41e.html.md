Gather with friends and family at Clayton’s new Enye Latin Table.

Corey Agopian

Contemporary Latin food … in Clayton? Yes, it’s a little jarring to see the sleek, new Enye Latin Table pumping _cumbia_ music, muddling mint, and swirling ancho chili sauce in this out-of-the-way city’s tranquil downtown, set amid bucolic oak trees and sun-baked hills at the foot of Mount Diablo.

But times have changed in the East Bay over the last few decades, and Clayton is no exception—something Kent Ipsen knows as well as anyone.

More than 50 years ago, Ipsen’s parents, Skip and Bev, opened the first Skipolini’s near what is now Enye. Eventually, Kent Ipsen took charge of the pizza operation—perhaps most famous for its 13-topping Prego pie that purportedly helps induce labor—expanding it to seven locations around northern California and Nevada.

![DM2506_021_DIG.jpg](data:image/png;base64,iVBORw0KGgoAAAANSUhEUgAAAAQAAAADCAQAAAAe/WZNAAAAEElEQVR42mM8U88ABowYDABAxQPltt5zqAAAAABJRU5ErkJggg==)

The restaurant features a sleek, modern setting.

Corey Agopian

And while Ipsen has faithfully carried out his parents’ vision of providing quality, family-friendly dining at affordable prices, he thought that Clayton was ready for something more.

“There are a lot of mom-and-pop owner-operators here, including myself with Skipolini’s,” Ipsen says. “And I felt from living in this community my entire life and seeing people leave to go to Walnut Creek or Alamo or Lafayette or Danville to explore more forward-pressing restaurant concepts, we could do that right here at home—give people a reason to stay and maybe call their friends to come out to our beautiful town.”

Enye Latin Table certainly delivers. From the neon Ñ at the entrance to the splashy design and bold colors within, everything about the restaurant screams new, modern, and adult—a stark shift from Skipolini’s rustic, woody charm.

It’s not that all ages aren’t welcome here. Doors open at 4 p.m., a nice accommodation for early-bird families and seniors alike, while the kitchen is happy to make kid-friendly menu changes. (No pepper jam in my boys’ carnitas tacos.)

Enye could fit easily into downtown Walnut Creek’s hip dinner scene, while saving locals the 20-minute drive along Ygnacio Valley Road. And in fact, the food, drinks, vibe, and service are so on-point, I wouldn’t be surprised if we see some reverse traffic.

![DM2506_018_DIG.jpg](data:image/png;base64,iVBORw0KGgoAAAANSUhEUgAAAAQAAAADCAQAAAAe/WZNAAAAEElEQVR42mM8U88ABowYDABAxQPltt5zqAAAAABJRU5ErkJggg==)

Enye serves a variety of pan-Latin dishes.

Corey Agopian

Around 15 tapas anchor executive chef Mike Fischetti’s user-friendly menu, along with a selection of salads, tacos, and a half dozen or so entrées, offering a pan-Latin journey through Spain and Central and South America. Spanish and South American wines dominate, while red and white sangria is served by the glass and pitcher, but we recommend starting with a craft cocktail. Mojitos, margaritas, and pisco sours are all featured on the tropical cocktail list and are executed with skill and flair.

Lit aflame tableside, the caipirinha en fuego makes a dramatic entrance, then goes down easy with a balanced mix of muddled lime, brown sugar, and cachaça. Complement your drink with an order of the chips and dips, a fun version consisting of multiple types of chips circled by guacamole and four colorful salsas.

The menu provides multiple points of dining entry, from drink-friendly small bites to ample big plates. Tacos are a sure bet. They’re offered three for $18. Enye’s tortillas are delicately chewy and provide a wonderful warm wrapping for the tender, savory carnitas and chicken al pastor—both of which we enjoyed.

![DM2506_023_DIG.jpg](data:image/png;base64,iVBORw0KGgoAAAANSUhEUgAAAAQAAAADCAQAAAAe/WZNAAAAEElEQVR42mM8U88ABowYDABAxQPltt5zqAAAAABJRU5ErkJggg==)

Enye’s tacos are among the standout plates.

Corey Agopian

The tapas section is the heart of the menu, however, and where the kitchen really shows off. The _pulpo a la parrilla_ is a visual stunner. A curl of grilled octopus is topped by vibrantly green sprouts, arriving on a bed of mahogany-red ancho chili sauce accented by airy dollops of avocado mousse.

The flame-charred octopus is exquisitely tender, although the mild combo of smoky-sweet sauce and avocado lacked punch. Persuaded by our waiter, we ordered the bean and cheese pupusas and were rewarded with one of our standout dishes of the night. The crisped-up pupusa is light and fork-tender, revealing a remarkably velvety, flavorful bean-cheese puree accented by a picante topping of curtido, slightly fermented Salvadoran slaw.

![DM2506_022_DIG.jpg](data:image/png;base64,iVBORw0KGgoAAAANSUhEUgAAAAQAAAADCAQAAAAe/WZNAAAAEElEQVR42mM8U88ABowYDABAxQPltt5zqAAAAABJRU5ErkJggg==)

Choose from a range of drink options at the stylish bar.

Corey Agopian

Similarly, the esquites corn ribs deliver the creamy comfort factor that has made this Mexican street snack a ubiquitous treat. Wedges of roasted corn on the cob are slathered with a delicious smoky, spicy chipotle aioli and sprinkled with plenty of crumbles of salty queso fresco. You’ll need a full deployment of napkins after digging into this dish. And while we loved the tartness of the stew-y posole verde it’s served upon, the crispy pork belly was our least favorite.

Fischetti, who worked as executive chef for five years at Va de Vi Bistro and Wine Bar and at the Pocket in Carmel with Ipsen, was recently awarded certified Angus beef national chef of the year. He backs up that title with his churrasco steak. A huge section of flank steak is grilled to tender-chewy perfection, sliced, topped with herbaceous chimichurri sauce, and served on a base of roasted potatoes and Gaucho sausage. It’s a stunner and for $39 could easily serve three or four diners if paired with a couple of other items.

That’s a common theme. Portion sizes are ample, and while prices aren’t cheap, they don’t feel exorbitant for the quality: Tapas are all under $20, with most entrées under $40.

The restaurant’s well-trained waitstaff was unfailingly friendly and accommodating, even providing (to our horror) free soda refills for our kids. For all of its urbane flashiness, Enye maintains a spirit of small-town generosity. Perhaps it’s not so different from Skipolini’s after all. _[enyelatintable.com](http://enyelatintable.com)._
