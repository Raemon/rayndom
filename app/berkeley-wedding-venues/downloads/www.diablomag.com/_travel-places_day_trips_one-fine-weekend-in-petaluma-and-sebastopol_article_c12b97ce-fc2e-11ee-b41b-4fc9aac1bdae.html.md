Brandon Souza of Fours Media 

You may have seen the headlines about mustard season in Northern California’s famous wine regions, during which bright yellow flowers blanket many of the vineyards. Spring is indeed a lovely time of year in Napa Valley, but there are alternative locales that offer just as much blooming beauty, often at a lower price and with a more relaxed vibe. Sonoma County, specifically **Sebastopol** and its environs, is one such destination, making for an easy spring jaunt while veering onto the road a bit less traveled.

* * *

### Prime Petaluma

The first stop on your trip up Highway 101 should be **Petaluma.** Originally home to Indigenous tribes before eventually becoming a prime hub for shipping and transport, Petaluma has a legacy of agricultural abundance (eggs and dairy), architecture, and industry. The town’s rich history is evident in the downtown area, full of classic brick buildings, art, and an astounding number of eateries. When planning where to go for lunch, consider one of the newcomers, like **Quiote,** which serves freshly pressed tortillas made from heirloom blue corn, or the cheerful **Stellina Alimentari** deli (a new sister spot of the popular Stellina Pronto), which is blowing everyone away with legit Italian sandwiches on house-made focaccia. **Pearl** is a standout for its sweet café vibe and seasonally driven menu with a Middle Eastern slant.

While meandering through downtown, duck into **Copperfield’s Books,** an independent bookstore that is still thriving in the Amazon age. Nearby is **Petaluma Seed Bank,** the first West Coast location launched by Baker Creek, a non-GMO, open-pollinated seed company based in Missouri, with a mission to preserve heirloom seeds; any gardener will rejoice in a visit.

If you are here in the evening, snag reservations at **Table Culture Provisions** for its outstanding four- or seven-course chef’s tasting menu. Stop by the new tiki spot in the heart of downtown, **Kapu Bar,** and enjoy a fun cocktail before or after dinner.

### Celebrating Sebastopol

Around 25 minutes north is Sebastopol, another small town with agricultural roots (here, it’s orchard fruit production). Spring in Sebastopol is particularly lovely, with apple blossoms in the orchards and budbreaks in the vineyards. Revel in the blooms at the **Apple Blossom Parade and Festival,** which has been held annually for the last 78 years in late April. Even if you miss that event, there are plenty of ways to savor the season’s bounty.

An excellent place to start is the **Barlow,** a market district that spans four blocks in the former Barlow family applesauce processing and canning facility. You can explore on your own, stopping for supplies and sustenance at the numerous shops and restaurants, or you can opt for the **Tasting Tour,** held every day of the week except Tuesday and Wednesday. Over the course of 90 minutes, you will get an inside peek into seven spots, with tastes along the way. These include the urban **Pax Wines,** and a sip of its delicate trousseau gris; **Il Fusti** to partake of its olive oil (make sure to try the harissa flavor); the impressive **JG Switzer,** which offers a lesson in wool felting; and **Fern Bar,** an airy cocktail bar and restaurant that’s on everyone’s must list for good reason.

### Agricultural Attractions

But don’t stay at the Barlow too long; you have a lot more adventuring (and sampling) to do. Your ultimate destination should be the **Farmhouse Inn,** a picturesque boutique hotel about 15 minutes from Sebastopol. This family operation, owned by siblings Catherine and Joe Bartolomei (in collaboration with the Foley Family, under the Foley Entertainment Group), is luxurious yet understated, modern yet rustic. The knowledge of these fifth-generation locals runs deep; the Bartolomeis partner with the surrounding community to provide insight and opportunity for guests in the form of curated outings, locally sourced ingredients and bath products, and a daily wine hour with area winemakers. Have your hosts book a day of wine tasting or strike out on your own. The inn is well-situated and connects with many fun stops along the scenic country roads.

![DM2404_017_DIG.jpg](data:image/png;base64,iVBORw0KGgoAAAANSUhEUgAAAAQAAAADCAQAAAAe/WZNAAAAEElEQVR42mM8U88ABowYDABAxQPltt5zqAAAAABJRU5ErkJggg==)

Try Ethic Ciders’ beverages at its new tasting room.

Courtesy of Ethic Ciders

Don’t skip **Ethic Ciders,** a Good Food Award–winning cider company that just opened a little tasting room at its orchard site, offering samples of its dry-farmed organic cider lineup as well as its apple brandy and pommeau (a French style apéritif that blends apple brandy with apple cider). In the latter case, Ethic uses its apple brandy, aged two years in American oak, and blends it with late-harvest apples, pears, and cider to add another layer of complexity.

Another thriving farmstead product in the region is cheese. **Bohemian Creamery** is a hot spot to sample goat cheeses, plus a variety of cow, sheep, and water buffalo milk cheeses (all made on-site). You can try three for free, or opt for a tasting flight of more than a dozen cheeses, along with some grilled focaccia. Grab a chunk of Boho Belle or Bo Peep to take home. For other delectable goods, make a stop at **Redwood Hill Farm,** a brand well-known for its goat milk products. The farm site is where it all began, though Redwood Hill now operates the Capracopia label. Fall in love with baby goats, then pick up top-quality olive oil and soap to take home. Redwood’s spring farm tour season runs from late March through the end of April.

![DM2404_020_DIG.jpg](data:image/png;base64,iVBORw0KGgoAAAANSUhEUgAAAAQAAAADCAQAAAAe/WZNAAAAEElEQVR42mM8U88ABowYDABAxQPltt5zqAAAAABJRU5ErkJggg==)

A tasting at Small Vines promises great sips and hospitality.

Dawn Heumann

No visit to the area is complete without at least one wine tasting, and a memorable place to book an appointment is **Small Vines.** Here you will get an education, learning from founders Kathryn and Paul Sloan about how they make their organic, regenerative, estate-grown pinot noir and chardonnay. Keep an eye out for their soon-to-be-released sparkling program.

Back at the Farmhouse Inn, take time to relax and refresh before dinner. You’ll want to be fully present for the inn’s six-course experience, helmed by executive chef Craig Wilmer, who came on in April 2023. The restaurant held a Michelin star for 14 years under the guidance of former chef Steve Litke; there is no doubt chef Wilmer’s seafood-centric menu will help snag Farmhouse at least one star in the future.

While there are a couple of hotel developments on the horizon in both Petaluma and Sebastopol, for now the top option for a place to spend the night in this region is Farmhouse Inn, or at elegant lodgings at nearby winery estates such as **Naidu Wines** or **Dalya Estate.** Regardless of where you end up, a springtime visit to this western span of Sonoma County will have you planning a return trip come summer, fall, and winter.
