**From small-town murders to supernatural happenings to action-filled escapades, good mysteries are irresistible. For decades, readers have flocked to the genre, enjoying familiar tropes—corpses, shadowy characters with something to hide, and the occasional cat—that have been twisted and crafted into stories that still surprise. This winter, settle in with a cup of tea and recent whodunits from East Bay authors Penny Warner, Juliet Blackwell, and Lisa Towles.**

* * *

![pennywarner_print_revised.jpg](data:image/png;base64,iVBORw0KGgoAAAANSUhEUgAAAAQAAAADCAQAAAAe/WZNAAAAEElEQVR42mM8U88ABowYDABAxQPltt5zqAAAAABJRU5ErkJggg==)

### Cozy Creator

**Penny Warner** ’s love of the mystery genre dates back to elementary school, when she read the iconic Nancy Drew series. She went on to write nonfiction books about raising kids while teaching child development to students at Diablo Valley College for 35 years. “Whenever I had extra time, I did some writing—mostly parenting books because that’s what I was teaching. I’d been reading a lot of murder mysteries by local writers and [thought], _I should try this,_ ” she says. “It was my third [mystery] that finally sold.”

![MURDER_BOOK.jpg](data:image/png;base64,iVBORw0KGgoAAAANSUhEUgAAAAQAAAADCAQAAAAe/WZNAAAAEElEQVR42mM8U88ABowYDABAxQPltt5zqAAAAABJRU5ErkJggg==)

The longtime Danville resident has now published more than 80 fiction and nonfiction books, including several California-set mystery series that follow a San Francisco event planner, a Bay Area restaurant-critic-turned–food-truck-employee, and a deaf reporter in Gold Country. The rights for the latter series have been optioned for movies and TV by Marlee Matlin. Warner favors cozy mysteries—a genre that includes books from writers such as Agatha Christie and typically steers clear of violence and sex—because of her own reading tastes. “I don’t want to write that,” Warner says. “I want it to be fun, a little humorous, a good puzzle, and [with] kind of quirky characters.”

In September, she dropped her latest mystery, _Murder at Blackwood Inn,_ kicking off a new series. The book follows Carissa, a troubled ghostwriter who moves in with her elderly aunts as they are opening a bed-and-breakfast that they’ve refurbished with a haunted theme. As with many cozy mysteries, the small-town setting (here reminiscent of Bodega Bay) and interwoven community are integral players in the plot. “We go to Bodega Bay a lot, and I thought, _This would be a perfect setting, like on the moors in an English mystery,_ ” Warner says. “A small town makes it a closed setting, [and] that small-town atmosphere gives you classic characters to work with.”

* * *

> #### “I don’t want to write [violence and sex]. I want [my mysteries] to be fun, a little humorous, a good puzzle, and [with] kind of quirky characters.”
> 
> ##### —Penny Warner

* * *

When a body is discovered after a party at the inn, Carissa attempts to clear her aunts’ names from suspicion of wrongdoing. Warner also includes a few recipes at the end of the book, a favorite feature among cozy readers but one that required some research. “I don’t usually make the recipes in other people’s books,” she says. “My husband is a good cook, so he tested them for me.”

Warner has also received acclaim for her kids’ series _The Code Busters Club,_ including two books released last summer. The series has earned multiple Agatha Awards, named for the Queen of Crime herself and regarded as among the most prestigious prizes for writers in the genre, and been published internationally. Warner even returned to the fictional River Heights of her childhood with _The Official Nancy Drew Handbook,_ which offers life advice to aspiring detectives.

Closer to home, Warner incorporates the greater Bay Area in her fiction time and time again. “Most of the cozies that I started reading were set in San Francisco. I wanted to bring it out to the suburbs. I think we have a lot of untapped places where we could murder people,” she says jokingly. “Carmel would be nice. Tahoe—I’ve got a whole list.”

* * *

![julietblackwell_print_revised.jpg](data:image/png;base64,iVBORw0KGgoAAAANSUhEUgAAAAQAAAADCAQAAAAe/WZNAAAAEElEQVR42mM8U88ABowYDABAxQPltt5zqAAAAABJRU5ErkJggg==)

### Ghost Hunter

**Juliet Blackwell** was working late on a job at an empty Pacific Heights mansion in San Francisco for her design and decorative painting studio when she and her assistant heard footsteps upstairs. “It’s like 2:30 in the morning, and we’re hearing things,” she says. “We finally went back to work. It was obviously our imaginations, but it got me thinking.”

![ASYLUM_BOOK.jpg](data:image/png;base64,iVBORw0KGgoAAAANSUhEUgAAAAQAAAADCAQAAAAe/WZNAAAAEElEQVR42mM8U88ABowYDABAxQPltt5zqAAAAABJRU5ErkJggg==)

The incident—and the realization that she had a unique job, much like many cozy detectives—gave rise to the longtime mystery reader’s first book, _Feint of Art._ Cowritten with her sister and published in 2006, the book was an Agatha Award nominee and spawned the larger Art Lover’s Mysteries series. Now a _New York Times_ bestselling author, Blackwell has also authored the Haunted Home Renovation Mystery books and Witchcraft Mystery series, which follow a woman running a construction company and a magically gifted vintage clothing store owner, both based in San Francisco. Blackwell is a Bay Area native and has been an Oakland resident since the late 1990s. 

“I almost always have protagonists living in Oakland. I adore Oakland. It has been very good to me,” she says. “I love the old buildings; I love the history. I think a lot of people don’t know it and don’t understand how wonderful it is.”

Blackwell’s newest novel, a standalone paranormal mystery titled _Asylum Hotel_ that dropped in July, takes her heroine to the area around Gualala. Aubrey, a photographer, encounters Dimitri while taking pictures at a dilapidated, abandoned hotel. When Dimitri’s body is found at the base of a cliff the next morning, Aubrey’s attempt to unveil the details around his death eventually involves the hotel’s history, curses, and spectral presences.

“There’s a whole lot that goes on in life we don’t fully comprehend, whether you see that as traditional ghosts or if it’s more just a feeling that you get when somebody’s watching you from across the room,” says Blackwell. “We have these sensations that are hard to explain. … When you go into a place that has borne witness to various human lives over the years, I feel like those buildings carry some sort of sensation.”

To develop the hotel’s vivid, eerie details, Blackwell relied on her visual arts background. “I didn’t realize until I started hanging out with other writers that not everybody sees their book as a movie,” she says. “I’m trying to describe what I’m seeing in my mind. As somebody who has been a painter, an artist, all my life—and then I was trained as an anthropologist; I was a social worker for a while—all of that trained me to observe people and places.”

Blackwell is following her paranormal, witchy theme with _The Séance Garden,_ a new standalone planned for release this year. The novel highlights a professor who teaches the history of the occult and wrestles with her own sense of disbelief—until she has a brush with the supernatural. Blackwell’s résumé also includes six traditional fiction novels set in France. As someone who has worked in both genres (and lived in countries around the globe), she thinks that the puzzle element of a mystery is what continues to hook readers—plus a desire for closure that real life doesn’t always allow.

“Most mysteries [are able to] identify a bad guy and unmask that villain at the end, and then the assumption is that villain will be appropriately punished,” she says. “I think that’s ultimately what it is—that attempt to make sense of the world, put the pieces together in a way that makes sense, and then to see that justice is served at the end.”

* * *

![lisatowles_print_revised.jpg](data:image/png;base64,iVBORw0KGgoAAAANSUhEUgAAAAQAAAADCAQAAAAe/WZNAAAAEElEQVR42mM8U88ABowYDABAxQPltt5zqAAAAABJRU5ErkJggg==)

### Thriller Seeker

The chance to explore what characters keep hidden drew **Lisa Towles** to crime thrillers. “There’s some secret that someone else doesn’t want to get out, and they will do everything they can to protect and insulate that secret from exposure,” she says. “[In all of my books], you have a topical crime, but then … you peel back the layers. At that bottom layer of the motivations [for the crimes] is a deeper mythology that relates to the main character’s personal life. I think that’s how things are, as human beings.”

![SWITCH_BOOK2.jpg](data:image/png;base64,iVBORw0KGgoAAAANSUhEUgAAAAQAAAADCAQAAAAe/WZNAAAAEElEQVR42mM8U88ABowYDABAxQPltt5zqAAAAABJRU5ErkJggg==)

Towles’s fourteenth book, _Switch,_ begins as Southern California private investigator Mari Ellwyn and her partner try to solve a spree of bank robberies but eventually uncover increasingly dangerous links to a body that has disappeared and a tech world espionage plot; the book also revisits shadowy figures from Ellwyn’s life. _Switch_ was released in September and is the conclusion to the E&A Investigations series, the previous installments of which became No. 1 Amazon Kindle bestsellers. “When I first created [Mari], I thought she had the potential to be literally a trainwreck of a human being. That’s what you want in a main character—no one wants to read about a main character who is happy and well adjusted and has their life together because that’s just not relatable,” says Towles. “I put her through a lot in these books.”

As her plot tumbles toward reckonings with Ellwyn’s past traumas and the mastermind behind current mysterious events, readers may find themselves flipping pages faster and faster as details are revealed. “Writers talk about either being a plotter or a pantser—if you’re a plotter, you meticulously outline your novels before you start writing … and pantsers just fly by the seat of their pants and plot the novel as [they’re] going along. I’m smack down the middle,” says Towles. “Sometimes my characters decide things, and I’m like, ‘Wait, how did we get here?’ But if that’s what feels right to the world that I’ve created, I stay true to that.”

* * *

> #### “No one wants to read about a main character who is happy and well adjusted and has their life together because that’s just not relatable.”
> 
> ##### —Lisa Towles

* * *

Towles’s passion for writing blossomed during her childhood in New England, when her mother would restrict her TV time to 30 minutes on snow days, encouraging her to engage in creative pursuits instead. Towles discovered that writing came very naturally, and now, as an adult who has spent the past 15-plus years living in Oakland, finds that her locale inspires her curiosity. “I feel so at home in California, but I’m always learning something new,” she says. “Oakland is amazing because you’re right at the center of everything.”

Towles’s novels are informed by her nearly two decades in the tech industry, where she has worked in technical writing and software engineering. She regularly juggles multiple projects and currently has several in the works. Fans can also check out her consulting company, Story Impact, where she offers book coaching; a podcast with author interviews; a magazine, which debuts in January; and a forthcoming workshop series. 

“Stories hold families, cultures, and countries together,” she says. “Especially with so much dividing us, stories are the unity.”
