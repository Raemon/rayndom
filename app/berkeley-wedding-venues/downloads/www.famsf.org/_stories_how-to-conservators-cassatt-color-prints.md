#  How-To with the Conservators: Mary Cassatt’s Color Prints  


By Christina Taylor, paper conservator at the Philadelphia Museum of Art

November 21, 2024 

![](data:image/gif;base64,R0lGODlhAQABAIAAAAAAAP///yH5BAEAAAAALAAAAAABAAEAAAIBRAA7) ![](https://www.famsf.org/storage/images/99cac3a8-f411-4b9f-b949-e8f96a1ea6b4/cassatt-the-letter-final.jpg?crop=2045,1152,x0,y709&format=jpg&quality=80&width=1000) ![](https://www.famsf.org/storage/images/99cac3a8-f411-4b9f-b949-e8f96a1ea6b4/cassatt-the-letter-final.jpg?crop=2045,1152,x0,y709&format=jpg&quality=80&width=1000)

##  Additional information 

[ ![](data:image/gif;base64,R0lGODlhAQABAIAAAAAAAP///yH5BAEAAAAALAAAAAABAAEAAAIBRAA7) ![Little girl laying in a blue armchair](https://www.famsf.org/storage/images/ba531e88-5047-4895-ba9b-e661438aa8cc/cassatt-little-girl-blue-armchair-1878.jpg?crop=4096,2731,x0,y0&format=jpg&quality=80&width=1000) ![Little girl laying in a blue armchair](https://www.famsf.org/storage/images/ba531e88-5047-4895-ba9b-e661438aa8cc/cassatt-little-girl-blue-armchair-1878.jpg?crop=4096,2731,x0,y0&format=jpg&quality=80&width=1000) About the exhibition  Mary Cassatt at Work  ](https://www.famsf.org/exhibitions/mary-cassatt)

Mary Cassatt’s “Set of Ten” (1891) prints are considered some of the most inventive in the history of modern printmaking. Using two to three metal plates, and a combination of soft ground, drypoint, and aquatint techniques, Cassatt created 25 copies of each scene in the set. Follow along with Christina Taylor, conservator of works of art on paper at the Philadelphia Museum of Art, to learn how Cassatt made her iconic color print, _The Letter_. 

## **Step 1: Drawing**

Cassatt started off by drawing her composition on paper.   


![](data:image/gif;base64,R0lGODlhAQABAIAAAAAAAP///yH5BAEAAAAALAAAAAABAAEAAAIBRAA7) ![](https://www.famsf.org/storage/images/745f9719-e145-41f9-9d0e-14c41562906a/mary-cassatt-the-letter-1890-1.jpg?crop=3536,5000,x0,y0&format=jpg&quality=80&width=1000) ![](https://www.famsf.org/storage/images/745f9719-e145-41f9-9d0e-14c41562906a/mary-cassatt-the-letter-1890-1.jpg?crop=3536,5000,x0,y0&format=jpg&quality=80&width=1000)

Mary Cassatt, _The Letter_(recto),__ 1890\. Black crayon and graphite, 34.6 x 23 cm (13 5/8 x 9 1/16 in.). Cleveland Museum of Art, Bequest of Charles T. Brooks. Image via Wikimedia  


![](data:image/gif;base64,R0lGODlhAQABAIAAAAAAAP///yH5BAEAAAAALAAAAAABAAEAAAIBRAA7) ![](https://www.famsf.org/storage/images/745f9719-e145-41f9-9d0e-14c41562906a/mary-cassatt-the-letter-1890-2.jpg?crop=1458,2047,x0,y0&format=jpg&quality=80&width=1000) ![](https://www.famsf.org/storage/images/745f9719-e145-41f9-9d0e-14c41562906a/mary-cassatt-the-letter-1890-2.jpg?crop=1458,2047,x0,y0&format=jpg&quality=80&width=1000)

Mary Cassatt, _The Letter_(verso),__ 1890\. Soft ground, 34.6 x 23 cm (13 5/8 x 9 1/16 in.). Cleveland Museum of Art, Bequest of Charles T. Brooks. Image via Wikimedia  


## **Step 2: Transferring the drawing**

After coating a metal plate in soft ground — a sticky, wax-based substance — Cassatt placed her drawing on top of the plate. She then traced over the drawing, applying pressure to lift soft ground from the plate beneath.  


![](data:image/gif;base64,R0lGODlhAQABAIAAAAAAAP///yH5BAEAAAAALAAAAAABAAEAAAIBRAA7) ![](https://www.famsf.org/storage/images/329c3623-e7cf-4a26-8c23-d672703de8df/mary-cassatt-printmaking-1.gif?crop=1162,626,x0,y0&format=jpg&quality=80&width=1000) ![](https://www.famsf.org/storage/images/329c3623-e7cf-4a26-8c23-d672703de8df/mary-cassatt-printmaking-1.gif?crop=1162,626,x0,y0&format=jpg&quality=80&width=1000)

Tracing over the drawing on paper, which is wrapped around a metal plate coated in soft ground; the pressure from the tracing lifts soft ground from the plate  


Cassatt then submerged the plate in an acid bath. Wherever there was acid-resistant soft ground, the plate was protected. Everywhere with bare metal — where the tracing was done — was etched lightly into the plate.   


![](data:image/gif;base64,R0lGODlhAQABAIAAAAAAAP///yH5BAEAAAAALAAAAAABAAEAAAIBRAA7) ![](https://www.famsf.org/storage/images/be5f319f-be95-438b-b03e-a4547fd1382d/mary-cassatt-printmaking-2.gif?crop=1152,620,x0,y0&format=jpg&quality=80&width=1000) ![](https://www.famsf.org/storage/images/be5f319f-be95-438b-b03e-a4547fd1382d/mary-cassatt-printmaking-2.gif?crop=1152,620,x0,y0&format=jpg&quality=80&width=1000)

Submerging the plate into the acid bath; the bare metal lines of the drawing are etched by the acid  


## **Step 3: Drawing lines with drypoint**

Cassatt used her etched plate as a guide to start her drypoint (a technique in which a sharp needle scratches directly into an uncoated metal plate). Scratching into the plate, she displaced some of the metal, creating raised burrs.  


![](data:image/gif;base64,R0lGODlhAQABAIAAAAAAAP///yH5BAEAAAAALAAAAAABAAEAAAIBRAA7) ![](https://www.famsf.org/storage/images/1c70f544-f7ca-4c9e-8fbc-2a2555aaf507/mary-cassatt-printmaking-3.gif?crop=1158,624,x0,y0&format=jpg&quality=80&width=1000) ![](https://www.famsf.org/storage/images/1c70f544-f7ca-4c9e-8fbc-2a2555aaf507/mary-cassatt-printmaking-3.gif?crop=1158,624,x0,y0&format=jpg&quality=80&width=1000)

Scratching lines into the metal plate  


Cassatt then inked the plate, pushing ink into the scratches and burrs while wiping away excess ink from the surface. Using a roller-style press, she printed the image onto paper. She then used this inked print to transfer the drawing onto two other plates, a process called counterproofing. The rich, velvety lines of her drypoint can be seen in early proofs of _The Letter_.   


![](data:image/gif;base64,R0lGODlhAQABAIAAAAAAAP///yH5BAEAAAAALAAAAAABAAEAAAIBRAA7) ![](https://www.famsf.org/storage/images/5b88851b-0e76-4182-a41f-f7dadada47c4/mary-cassatt-printmaking-gif-4.gif?crop=1860,999,x0,y0&format=jpg&quality=80&width=1000) ![](https://www.famsf.org/storage/images/5b88851b-0e76-4182-a41f-f7dadada47c4/mary-cassatt-printmaking-gif-4.gif?crop=1860,999,x0,y0&format=jpg&quality=80&width=1000)

Wiping ink from the surface of the plate; ink in scratches and burrs remain to create the lines of the image  


## **Step 4: Adding tone + color with aquatint**

Cassatt used the counterproofed image on the plates as guides for her aquatint (a technique in which areas of tone are added to a print). In order to create a specific shape in aquatint, Cassatt applied an acid-resistant shellac to protect certain areas of the plate during etching. For example, she used this technique around the flowers in _The Letter_.  


![](data:image/gif;base64,R0lGODlhAQABAIAAAAAAAP///yH5BAEAAAAALAAAAAABAAEAAAIBRAA7) ![](https://www.famsf.org/storage/images/e237ae23-606b-4c8c-a58b-ce62b7c7353d/mary-cassatt-printmaking-5.gif?crop=1156,622,x0,y0&format=jpg&quality=80&width=1000) ![](https://www.famsf.org/storage/images/e237ae23-606b-4c8c-a58b-ce62b7c7353d/mary-cassatt-printmaking-5.gif?crop=1156,622,x0,y0&format=jpg&quality=80&width=1000)

Painting shellac around areas to be etched  


Once dry, she dusted rosin (dried tree sap) evenly over the plate and fused it to the plate with heat. Again, she placed the plates in an acid bath, etching the metal around the rosin and creating textured areas to hold ink.   


![](data:image/gif;base64,R0lGODlhAQABAIAAAAAAAP///yH5BAEAAAAALAAAAAABAAEAAAIBRAA7) ![](https://www.famsf.org/storage/images/d1646472-632b-4a8e-ad1a-8e3c544e285d/mary-cassatt-printmaking-fusing.gif?crop=,,x,y&format=jpg&quality=80&width=1000) ![](https://www.famsf.org/storage/images/d1646472-632b-4a8e-ad1a-8e3c544e285d/mary-cassatt-printmaking-fusing.gif?crop=,,x,y&format=jpg&quality=80&width=1000)

Fusing the rosin to the plate with heat; a direct flame is held beneath the plate  


## **Step 5: Inking + printing**

Cassatt applied colored ink to the etched areas of her three plates. Adjacent colors that Cassatt did not want to be mixed (such as the brown of the desk and blue of the dress) were applied to different plates, and different colors were applied to the same plate using a technique called à la poupée. You can see in _The Letter_ that Cassatt inked the blue dress on the same plate as the pink and green flowers, since some blue can be seen mixing into the flowers around the dress.  


![](data:image/gif;base64,R0lGODlhAQABAIAAAAAAAP///yH5BAEAAAAALAAAAAABAAEAAAIBRAA7) ![](https://www.famsf.org/storage/images/a6e59f41-96b5-4b55-b98f-925230ac68a9/mary-cassatt-printmaking-7.gif?crop=1162,620,x0,y0&format=jpg&quality=80&width=1000) ![](https://www.famsf.org/storage/images/a6e59f41-96b5-4b55-b98f-925230ac68a9/mary-cassatt-printmaking-7.gif?crop=1162,620,x0,y0&format=jpg&quality=80&width=1000)

Adding different colors of ink to one plate, à la poupée  


After wiping away excess ink, Cassatt printed all three plates in perfect registration onto one sheet of paper to create her final print.  


![](data:image/gif;base64,R0lGODlhAQABAIAAAAAAAP///yH5BAEAAAAALAAAAAABAAEAAAIBRAA7) ![](https://www.famsf.org/storage/images/3e7b44f5-eaa9-4ffe-8cfc-f0f0557a4af5/mary-cassatt-letter-state-1.jpg?crop=,,x,y&format=jpg&quality=80&width=1000) ![](https://www.famsf.org/storage/images/3e7b44f5-eaa9-4ffe-8cfc-f0f0557a4af5/mary-cassatt-letter-state-1.jpg?crop=,,x,y&format=jpg&quality=80&width=1000)

Mary Cassatt, _The Letter_ , 1890–1891. Printer Modeste Leroy. Drypoint, printed in black ink from one plate. 13 5/8 x 9 1/8 in. (34.6 x 23.2 cm). The Metropolitan Museum of Art, Gift of Arthur Sachs, 1916  


![](data:image/gif;base64,R0lGODlhAQABAIAAAAAAAP///yH5BAEAAAAALAAAAAABAAEAAAIBRAA7) ![](https://www.famsf.org/storage/images/99cac3a8-f411-4b9f-b949-e8f96a1ea6b4/cassatt-the-letter-final.jpg?crop=,,x,y&format=jpg&quality=80&width=1000) ![](https://www.famsf.org/storage/images/99cac3a8-f411-4b9f-b949-e8f96a1ea6b4/cassatt-the-letter-final.jpg?crop=,,x,y&format=jpg&quality=80&width=1000)
