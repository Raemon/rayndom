# Unraveling Canine Parvovirus: A Comprehensive Guide for Pet Owners

[Dr. Karen Whala](/experts/dr-karen-whala)

September 18, 2023

Canine Parvovirus is a serious and potentially deadly disease. While this disease is fairly common, it is also preventable. In this guide, we will explore what parvo is, its symptoms, diagnosis, treatment, and most importantly, how to prevent it. Sadly, many puppies who are diagnosed with parvovirus are euthanized as hospitalization can present a financial hardship and survival depends upon the dog’s ability to fight illness with supportive care. While not intended to be a substitute for veterinary care, it is our goal to stress the importance of prevention and help equip you with the knowledge to protect your pet and ensure their well-being.

## **Understanding Canine Parvovirus**

“Parvo” as the disease caused by Canine Parvovirus (CPV) is often called, can be a frightening prospect and an even more terrifying diagnosis. We're here to break down this complex health issue into manageable terms.

### Decoding Canine Parvovirus

CPV, a highly contagious virus, poses a significant health risk primarily to puppies.lt’s known for causing an acute gastrointestinal illness, but isn't confined to the younger dog population. Adult dogs can be susceptible as well as wild canids and other wildlife such as foxes, raccoons, and skunks. The virus infects its victims through direct or indirect contact with the feces of an infected animal.

Parvo primarily affects the stomach and intestines, leading to symptoms like fever, lethargy, weight loss, vomiting, and severe- often bloody- diarrhea. An infected dog may start shedding the virus 4-5 days after exposure, even before symptoms are visible. And virus particles are shed in large quantities, making the spread of the virus quite insidious.

### The Evolution of Canine Parvovirus

It is believed that the current Canine Parvovirus traces its origins back to mutations of the feline panleukopenia virus (FPV). As the virus emerged in the late 1970s, it spread swiftly affecting dogs across the globe. Since then this highly contagious pathogen has been slowed, thanks to prevention efforts but it remains prevalent.

### Reasons for its Spread

Unfortunately, parvo has the ability of lingering in the environment for long periods of time which is one of the primary reasons for its continued spread. The virus can survive indoors for at least a month. And under proper conditions it can remain viable outdoors for up to a year. The virus primarily spreads through the feces of an infected dog, which can contain extremely large amounts of virus particles even in small samples.

### How is Parvovirus Transmitted?

Parvovirus, a highly contagious virus that affects dogs, primarily spreads through direct or indirect contact with contaminated feces. The infectious particles are considered nearly ubiquitous and can be picked up on a dog's paws, coat, or even a person's shoes and clothing, leading to transmission. Intriguingly, dogs can start shedding the virus approximately 4 to 5 days post-exposure, potentially infecting others before any symptoms appear. This sneak-attack characteristic of Parvovirus makes it particularly challenging to control.  
  
Environmental resilience further complicates matters. Parvovirus can persist indoors for at least one month and has the capacity to survive outdoors for up to an entire year, depending on conditions. Recognizing these persistent and stealthy transmission vectors is essential for pet owners in the prevention efforts to protect their canine companions.

## **Recognizing Symptoms of Canine Parvovirus**

### Spotting the Physical Signs

Identifying the physical symptoms of canine parvovirus is helpful for early detection and intervention. This insidious virus leads to gastrointestinal illness causing severe vomiting and diarrhea, often resulting in dehydration and weight loss. The diarrhea may be especially foul-smelling and might contain blood due to damage of the intestinal walls. Other common physical symptoms include lethargy and a loss of appetite. The infected dog may also exhibit a high fever.

### Severity of Symptoms

The severity of symptoms in canine parvovirus can vary, often depending on factors such as the age, viral burden, and overall health status of the dog. The severity of a dog’s illness can be exacerbated by secondary infections or the presence of intestinal parasites, leading to a significantly more debilitated condition.

Severely affected dogs may be unable to keep food and water down leading to progressive weakness and continued gastrointestinal distress. If untreated, the dog can rapidly develop severe dehydration, low cell counts, sepsis from systemic bacterial infection, shock, and death. Immediate veterinary attention is crucial to early intervention which can improve overall prognosis.

### Duration and Progression

The progression and duration of symptoms in canine parvovirus can vary based on the severity of the case and the timeliness of treatment. Once a dog is infected, there is an incubation period of three to seven days before symptoms occur.

In early stages the virus invades and replicates in the tonsils and lymphatic tissues of the throat and neck before entering white blood cells. From inside the white blood cells, the virus gains access to the rest of the body and enters the rapidly dividing cells of the bone marrow and the intestines. From within the bone marrow the virus causes disruptions to the immune system and cell development. Meanwhile from within the intestine, the virus impairs the body's ability to replenish the intestinal surface leading to vomiting, diarrhea, malabsorption of nutrients, fluid loss from the tissues, and sets the pup up for bacterial invasion- from bacteria normally found in the gut moving into the tissues and blood stream..

Parvo can progress rapidly — going from the first signs of illness to severe, life-threatening disease within 48 to 72 hours. However, most dogs that survive the first few days of treatment will usually make a full recovery and go on to lead happy, healthy lives. Recovery times vary greatly depending on the extent of the disease and potential secondary conditions but most pups who will recover do so in 5-10 days.

## **Breeds at Risk for Parvovirus**

While Parvovirus can affect any dog, studies have shown certain breeds such as Rottweilers, Doberman Pinschers, German Shepherds, English Springer Spaniels, and American Pit Bull Terriers may have a higher susceptibility to this disease. Owners of these breeds should be especially watchful for signs of Parvo and stay diligent with vaccinations.

## **Diagnosing Canine Parvovirus**

Understanding how veterinarians diagnose canine parvovirus can help you be prepared and informed for what lies ahead. While the first step in most veterinary visits is a comprehensive physical examination, your vet may actually begin with testing to help triage and isolate your pup if they are positive for parvo. We will discuss testing in more detail below, but first we will look at the exam.

### How is Parvovirus Detected?

In puppies of susceptible age and vaccine status, the simplest and earliest diagnostic ‘test’ is whether they are eating or not. Though definitely not fool-proof, if a puppy is not eating, many in the vet field believe this strongly suggests ‘parvo’ until proven otherwise, also known jokingly as the ‘5 second Parvo test’. In more definitively diagnosing Canine Parvovirus (CPV), veterinarians commonly employ a fecal ELISA test. Conducted at the clinic, this test provides results promptly, typically within 15 minutes. It requires a small sample from the dog's tonsils and stool, offering a practical way to detect the virus without necessitating blood draws. Moreover, ELISA tests are favored for their cost-efficiency and accessibility.

To augment the fecal ELISA, Polymerase Chain Reaction (PCR) testing can be utilized to detect CPV DNA in a dog's stool. While PCR tests are inherently more sensitive and can confirm the presence of the virus even with minute amounts, they necessitate sending samples to a specialized lab, resulting in a longer wait for results.

Veterinarians may also advocate for a complete blood count (CBC). This test is integral to monitoring the effect Parvovirus may have on a dog's hematopoietic system. Parvo often leads to a decline in both red and white blood cell counts, since it attacks the bone marrow, where these cells are produced. By analyzing the CBC, the veterinarian can assess the severity of the infection and its impact on the dog's overall health.

### Veterinary Examination

A good physical exam is the mainstay for diagnosis, monitoring disease progression, and responses to treatment. Your veterinarian will consider your pet's history, including their age, vaccination status, and potential exposure to the disease. Signalment (age, sex, breed, and reproductive status) will help bring certain diseases higher on the list of possible causes. 

Parvo disproportionately affects puppies aged six weeks to six months. In addition certain breeds are most susceptible to parvo, such as Rottweilers, Doberman Pinschers, German Shepherds, English Springer Spaniels, and American Pit Bull Terriers, to name a few.

During this examination, your vet will check for the severity of your pet’s illness as well as key symptoms of the disease, such as:

  * Vomiting
  * Fever
  * Lethargy
  * Loss of appetite
  * Weight loss
  * Dehydration



Keep in mind that while these symptoms may occur with parvovirus they can also be indicative of many other illnesses. Fortunately, diagnosis can often be simple and quickly obtained.

### Laboratory Tests

As stated before, tabletop tests may be the first step in the process. There are a few diagnostic methods that can be used but the most common is a fecal ELISA test. The ELISA (enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay) test can be run in about 15 minutes using a small swab from the dogs tonsils and feces. 

The test cartridge holds onto the virus particles, when present in the sample, and causes color changes indicating a positive test; similar to a home COVID or pregnancy test. This test is valuable as it is quick, relatively inexpensive, and can be performed easily without collecting blood samples. This is a useful tool for testing right away and placing the pet in the appropriate area of the clinic or hospital to avoid spreading parvo.

Although ELISA is the most commonly used test to diagnose parvo, PCR tests can also diagnose parvo by identifying the presence of CPV DNA. PCR tests require samples to be sent to a reference laboratory, taking longer to obtain results.

Additionally, your vet will likely want to run complete blood counts (CBC) to monitor red and white blood cell levels which can be diminished due to the virus’ effect on bone marrow.

## **Treatment for Canine Parvovirus**

Beginning treatment as early as possible is important in most diseases, and CPV is no exception. Unfortunately, there is no treatment targeted to combat the virus itself. Like many other viral infections, treatment consists of supportive and symptomatic care. As such the length and intensity of treatment will depend on the individual and their ability to mount an immune response and fight off the virus.

### Importance of Early Intervention

The early stage of the infection is a critical time. Parvovirus is a fast-spreading disease, and its severity escalates quickly within a dog's system. The moment you notice your pet displaying signs like lethargy, fever, or gastrointestinal distress, schedule an immediate appointment with your veterinarian. Dogs treated within 48 to 72 hours of symptom onset tend to have better survival rates compared to those who don’t receive timely treatment, highlighting just how important early intervention truly is.

### Hospitalization and Supportive Care

Hospitalization provides the pup’s best chance for care that is quickly responsive to their changing needs.During hospitalization, the supportive care provided is aimed at boosting your pet’s immune system, maintaining their nutritional balance, and managing the debilitating symptoms. Intravenous (IV) fluids are typically administered to rectify severe dehydration and electrolyte imbalances due to ongoing losses from vomiting, diarrhea, and possible “leaky” gut. The amount of IV fluids can be adjusted as needed throughout the dog’s recovery. 

Nutritional support is also a crucial piece of supportive care. Despite vomiting and diarrhea, some studies show that careful feeding helps the gastrointestinal cells heal faster. Veterinary professionals may hand feed bland prescription diets or may recommend the placement of a feeding tube to deliver nutrition to the ailing gut. Your veterinarian will assess the whole clinical picture for your pet and determine when and what to feed your dog throughout the hospitalization and recovery period.

### Medication and Treatment Plans

In addition to supportive care, symptomatic care is a mainstay of parvoviral treatment. Anti-nausea medications will likely be a part of the treatment plan and possibly antibiotics. Broad-spectrum antibiotics may be necessary if secondary bacterial infection, a common complication from the virus, is suspected. In severe cases, patients may require blood or plasma transfusions as well as ongoing monitoring of blood values. As you can see, the treatment plans are tailored to your pet's individual needs and may vary over the course of the healing process.

### Nutrition and Hydration Management

A crucial part of managing Parvovirus is ensuring that your pet maintains proper nutrition and hydration, even amidst symptoms like vomiting and diarrhea. Current understanding within veterinary medicine suggests that careful feeding may aid in the recuperation of the gastrointestinal cells, thus enhancing healing. To provide nutritional support, veterinarians may introduce bland, easily-digestible prescription diets, sometimes via hand-feeding or using a feeding tube for more severe cases.

### How Much Does Treatment for Parvo Cost?

Costs will vary greatly depending on how many days of hospitalization are needed, how intensive the necessary care is, and the average costs in your region.

Hospital stays for parvo patients usually last about one week and most hospitals charge by the day. Some hospitals will also break down charges into categories of more intensive care to less intensive care with the costs for specific medications added as needed. The severity of each case will play a role as complications, such as secondary bacterial infections, will need to be monitored and treated, increasing costs.

Generally speaking, the costs for treating an uncomplicated course of parvo is $1,000-$1,500. Again this is a ballpark range as cost varies widely due to multiple factors. Suffice it to say that it is well worth the price of vaccinating your dog against this virulent and expensive virus.

Outpatient treatment is not recommended as a dog’s condition can change rapidly and adjustments must be made quickly. However, in some cases outpatient treatment may be successful and is preferable to euthanasia upon diagnosis (unless the dog is already in the [dying process](https://www.codapet.com/a-complete-guide-to-pet-euthanasia/how-to-tell-if-my-dog-is-dying)). Outpatient treatment would involve bringing the dog to the veterinary clinic to be reassessed and receive treatments, possibly multiple times daily.

### Recovering and Prognosis

It's important to be patient and give your pet time to recover. Complete healing may take a week or longer in severe cases. The overall prognosis for canine parvovirus is hopeful, if early intervention and the proper treatment methods are implemented. Fortunately, 70-90% of dogs with CPV enteritis who receive appropriate supportive care will survive (1). 

Dogs who do not survive parvo usually succumb to shock, or toxemia from bacterial infection. Euthanasia is sometimes pursued if dogs are not “turning a corner” early in treatment or upon diagnosis, if treatment is not an option. When selected, euthanasia is usually performed in the clinic rather than at-home as the pet is usually unstable for transfer.

Dogs who have survived parvo will develop long term immunity. Even so, they should still receive the recommended core vaccines. This is because the immunity may not be life long and may not protect against all viral strains. Additionally the parvo vaccine often includes inoculations against other diseases, which your dog would still be susceptible to if not given the combination vaccine.

## **Preventing Canine Parvovirus**

As pet owners, our primary goal is to ensure the health and safety of our beloved companions. One of the most critical steps towards this goal is the prevention of diseases like canine parvovirus. Here are some key steps you can take to prevent your precious pooch from contracting this disease.

### Importance of Vaccination

The best way to prevent canine parvovirus is through timely and complete vaccination. Puppies initially receive protection against parvo from their mother’s colostrum, assuming she was immunized and they were able to nurse. The maternal antibodies inside the puppy wane as the puppy’s immune system begins ramping up. 

Vaccines help to prime the immune system and protect against parvovirus, however as long as mom’s antibodies remain in the puppy’s system their own immune system will not effectively ‘learn’ from the vaccines. Because mom’s antibodies wane at different times for each dog the vaccine must be repeated at intervals for their first several months of life.

Most veterinarians recommend that a puppy receive their initial dose of the vaccine at 6 to 8 weeks of age, followed by a booster every three to four weeks until they are 16-20 weeks old. Then they should receive a booster one year later, and every three years thereafter. It is helpful to note, the inoculation for parvovirus is a combination vaccine and is often referred to as a “Distemper” shot. While canine distemper is a separate disease, the vaccine immunizes against both distemper and parvo, as well as other diseases. Speak with your veterinarian to be sure you understand exactly which diseases are being vaccinated against.

The importance of vaccination cannot be overstated- while no vaccine is perfect, parvoviral vaccines provide excellent protection. Until dogs are fully vaccinated, it is important that they avoid interaction with unvaccinated dogs or visit locations where parvo may be present (boarding and training facilities, dog parks,kennels, etc.). Speak with your veterinarian to determine when your dog’s vaccination protocol is considered complete and socialization may begin.

### Isolation and Quarantine

In case your dog has contracted parvo, it's vital to isolate them from other susceptible pets. This is because the virus is highly contagious and can spread rapidly. An infected dog begins shedding the virus four to five days after exposure, before showing symptoms, and this shedding can continue for up to 10 days after symptoms stop.

Hence, it's advisable to quarantine recovering puppies from unvaccinated and partially vaccinated dogs. Additionally, limit exposure to public spaces, such as parks or kennels, during the recovery period. If your home shares an outdoor area that is frequented by other families with dogs avoid this space until your dog is no longer shedding virus. Remember that under some circumstances the virus can survive up to a year outdoors. You may wish to put down puppy pads and train your dog to eliminate on these disposable surfaces.

Other pets may be at risk too. In some cases, cats may contract mild infections from the canine parvovirus, however there is likely some protective benefit from the feline “distemper” vaccine (FVRCP). Ferrets are also susceptible to canine parvo. Speak to your veterinarian about the risks to other pets and remember to isolate recovering pets whenever possible.

### Sanitation and Hygiene

The parvovirus is a very robust virus. As discussed before, it can survive indoors for a month and outdoors for several months up to a year. Additionally, the virus is shed in large quantities. Therefore, it is essential to maintain good hygiene and sanitation levels in any areas the infected pet has been.

Regular cleaning and disinfection of the environment are vital in controlling parvo. Make sure to clean and disinfect all objects that have been in contact with the infected dog. This includes bedding, food dishes, water bowls, leashes, and your clothing (don’t forget about shoes!).

Use a parvovirus-specific disinfectant or a solution of one part bleach to 30 parts water to disinfect the home and the pet's belongings. Remember, ordinary household cleaners might not be sufficient to eliminate the virus. It is important to clear away organic matter before disinfecting as the disinfectants become ineffective in biologic matter such as feces and vomit. Grass and other organic matter can pose an added concern when trying to sanitize yards. In these cases it is not feasible to disinfect yard spaces with plants unless you plan on ripping out sod and starting over. In these instances dilution and sunlight can help reduce the viral load. This is another reason to keep your recovering pet away from these spaces.

### Regular Health Check-ups

Regular health check-ups with your veterinarian will allow for early detection of [diseases](https://www.codapet.com/senior-pet-care/5-common-terminal-dog-diseases) like canine parvovirus. Through these check-ups, your vet can monitor your pet's health and catch any potential issues before they escalate. In fact, here are [5 Reasons To Take Your Healthy Pet To The Vet](https://www.codapet.com/senior-pet-care/5-reasons-to-take-your-healthy-pet-to-the-vet) regularly to ensure they stay in peak condition.

Your veterinarian will update you on the recommended vaccinations and provide advice on hygiene and sanitation. Regular health check-ups are a proactive way of preventing a wide range of diseases, including parvovirus.

### Safe Socialization Practices for Puppies

There is some controversy over what is considered safe socialization practices for puppies. Ideally puppies should not be exposed to high risk areas prior to completing their vaccination series in the hopes of avoiding Parvo infection by 16-20 weeks of age. However, the window for adequate socialization and the development of a well-behaved puppy closes prior to 20 weeks. Balancing the risk of Parvo and the risks of missing the social development window are vital. If a pet owner would like to enroll their puppy in puppy classes AND minimize the risk of parvo, it is vital to initiate and maintain your puppy’s vaccination series on time and choose a puppy socialization class that understands and practices the importance of maintaining environmental hygiene and enforces vaccination of all attending puppies. It is believed that Parvovirus contaminated environments may pose highest risk in areas such as dog parks, kennels, and pet stores.  


By taking these precautions, you minimize the risk of your puppy contracting this potent virus while ensuring that your puppy can still benefit from social interactions without jeopardizing their well-being.

## **Living with a Parvovirus Survivor**

### Continued Health Monitoring

Living with a Parvovirus survivor requires ongoing health monitoring to ensure their full recovery. Regular vet check-ups, careful observation of any changes in their behavior or physical condition, and maintaining a healthy diet and lifestyle are all crucial aspects of this post-recovery care. Thankfully, after a puppy or dog fully recovers, they are believed to be immune for life and not likely to succumb to a parvovirus infection ever again.

### Possible Long-Term Effects of Parvovirus

While most dogs that survive Parvo are able to resume normal, healthy lives, some may experience lasting health effects. Complications can range from chronic gastrointestinal problems to a predisposition for heart issues, and in some cases, a persistently weakened immune system. Given these potential long-term effects, vigilant health monitoring becomes key to promptly identify and manage any issues that arise.

### Supporting Emotional Well-being of Your Pet

Surviving Parvo can be a stressful experience for your pet, which makes supporting their emotional well-being just as important as managing their physical health. A nurturing environment, gentle care, and plenty of love and affection can go a long way in promoting the emotional well-being of your pet.

## **Parvovirus is Manageable with Proper Care**

### Summary of Key Points

Undoubtedly, Canine Parvovirus is a concern for pet owners, but with vigilant care, it can be managed effectively. Understanding Parvo's nature and how it spreads is imperative in safeguarding your pet. The disease, while serious, often yields to early detection and prompt, diligent treatment — actions that can significantly uplift your pet's chances for recovery. Regular veterinary check-ups and a watchful eye on any behavioral or physical changes in your pet are crucial.

For our beloved companions who have braved through Parvo, their journey to health remains a priority. Ongoing health monitoring plays a pivotal role in their continued recovery, helping to identify and address any lasting impacts of the illness.

As pet owners, we have a responsibility to protect our pets from diseases like Parvo.
