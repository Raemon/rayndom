# How Veterinarians Assess Quality of Life vs. How Owners Do

[Codapet](/experts/codapet)

September 11, 2025

Pet Health

Quality of life assessment is one of the hardest parts of pet ownership, especially when facing chronic illness or end-of-life decisions. Veterinarians rely on clinical tools and medical expertise, while pet owners bring unique insights into their companion’s daily behaviors and personality. When these perspectives come together, families can make more compassionate, informed choices that honor both their pet’s comfort and their bond.

## **Key Takeaways**

  * Veterinarians use objective, clinical assessment tools while pet owners rely primarily on emotional connections and behavioral observations. Understanding these different approaches can help bridge communication gaps and lead to better decision-making for your pet's well-being.
  * Both perspectives are valuable and necessary for a comprehensive quality of life evaluation. Veterinarians provide medical expertise and objective criteria, while owners offer intimate knowledge of their pet's personality, preferences, and subtle changes that may not be apparent during clinical examinations.
  * Effective quality of life assessment requires collaboration between veterinary professionals and pet owners. Combining clinical expertise with owner observations creates the most complete picture of a pet's well-being and leads to more informed decisions about treatment options and end-of-life care.



Quality of life assessment represents one of the most challenging aspects of veterinary medicine and pet ownership, especially when beloved pets face chronic illness, aging-related decline, or terminal diagnoses. The way veterinarians evaluate a pet's well-being often differs significantly from how owners perceive their companion's quality of life, leading to confusion or difficult conversations about euthanasia solutions. These differences reflect distinct professional training and emotional relationships, providing valuable insights into the assessment process. Understanding how both veterinarians and pet owners approach quality of life evaluation can improve communication, reduce anxiety during these difficult choices, and ultimately lead to better outcomes for dogs and cats facing health challenges.

## **The Veterinary Perspective: Clinical Assessment Tools**

Veterinarians employ systematic, evidence-based methodologies for evaluating the quality of life, particularly in terminal pets such as dogs and cats. These approaches minimize emotional bias, ensuring that the euthanasia decision is based on measurable parameters and professional training in recognizing signs of distress or declining function.

### **Standardized Quality of Life Scales**

Most veterinary professionals utilize established quality-of-life assessment tools that provide structured frameworks for evaluation. The HHHHHMM Scale, developed by Dr. Alice Villalobos, remains one of the most widely used instruments in veterinary practice. This scale evaluates seven key areas: Hurt (pain management), Hunger (appetite and nutrition), Hydration (water intake and kidney function), Hygiene (ability to maintain cleanliness), Happiness (mental well-being and engagement), Mobility (movement and exercise capacity), and More good days than bad.

Each category receives a numerical score, typically from 1-10, with higher scores indicating better quality of life. Veterinarians use this systematic approach to create baseline measurements and track changes over time, providing objective data that can guide treatment decisions and help families understand their pet's condition progression.

### **Clinical Indicators and Physical Examination**

Veterinary assessment relies heavily on physical examination findings and clinical indicators that require professional training to interpret accurately. Veterinarians evaluate respiratory effort, heart rate patterns, body condition scoring, muscle mass assessment, and neurological function during routine examinations.

Pain assessment represents a critical component of veterinary quality of life evaluation. Veterinarians are trained to recognize subtle pain behaviors such as changes in posture, facial expressions, movement patterns, and response to touch that may not be obvious to pet owners. They also understand how various medical conditions typically progress and can anticipate pain or discomfort before it becomes severe.

### **Evidence-Based Decision Making**

Veterinarians approach quality of life assessment within the context of medical prognosis and treatment options. Their evaluation considers not only current status but also likely disease progression, response to treatment, and realistic expectations for improvement or stabilization.

The clinical approach emphasizes preventing future suffering rather than simply managing current symptoms. This forward-thinking perspective sometimes leads veterinarians to recommend earlier intervention or end-of-life discussions than owners might expect, based on professional understanding of disease progression patterns.

## **The Owner Perspective: Emotional and Behavioral Assessment**

Pet owners approach quality of life assessment for their beloved pets, such as dogs and cats, from a fundamentally different perspective, rooted in intimate knowledge of their companion's personality and preferences. This emotional connection provides valuable insights that clinical examination cannot capture, though it may also introduce uncertainties that complicate objective evaluation.

### **Intimate Knowledge and Behavioral Observation**

Pet owners possess unparalleled insight into their companion's personality, preferences, and subtle behavioral patterns that may not be apparent during brief veterinary visits. They understand what activities bring their pet joy, recognize early signs of discomfort or anxiety, and can detect changes in routine behaviors that might indicate declining well-being.

Owners also observe their pets continuously rather than during brief clinical encounters, allowing them to recognize patterns, good days versus bad days, and gradual changes that might not be evident during veterinary examinations. This longitudinal observation provides valuable context for understanding the pet's overall experience.

### **Emotional Attachment Challenges**

The deep emotional bond between pets and their owners significantly influences how owners perceive their companions' quality of life. This emotional connection can provide motivation for excellent care and advocacy for the pet's needs, but it may also create challenges in maintaining objective assessment.

Pet owners often interpret their companion's behaviors through the lens of their relationship and shared history. The emotional investment in the pet's well-being can lead to both over-treatment and under-treatment scenarios, with some owners pursuing aggressive treatments beyond what might be appropriate. In contrast, others may delay necessary interventions due to difficulty accepting their pet's declining condition.

## **Common Areas of Disagreement**

Understanding where veterinary and owner perspectives commonly diverge can help both parties communicate more effectively, especially when making difficult choices regarding terminal pets and their life quality.

### **Pain Assessment and Management**

One of the most frequent areas of disagreement involves **pain recognition** and management. Pets instinctively hide pain as a survival mechanism, making it challenging for owners to recognize when their companion is uncomfortable. Veterinarians understand this tendency and look for specific clinical signs, changes in movement patterns, and physiological indicators that suggest pain even when pets appear relatively normal to their families.

Disagreements often arise regarding pain medication use, with some owners concerned about side effects or believing their pet isn't experiencing significant discomfort. At the same time, veterinarians may recommend more aggressive pain management based on clinical assessment.

### **Mobility and Independence Standards**

Veterinarians and owners often have different standards for acceptable mobility and independence levels. Owners may be willing to provide extensive assistance with basic functions like eating, drinking, and elimination, viewing this support as expressing love and commitment to their pet.

Veterinary professionals consider a pet's ability to maintain basic dignity and independence as important quality-of-life factors. They may recommend intervention when pets lose the ability to position themselves for elimination, move without significant difficulty, or access food and water independently.

### **Treatment Intensity and Goals**

Disagreements about appropriate treatment intensity often reflect different goals and expectations. Owners may pursue aggressive treatments based on emotional attachment and hope for improvement, while veterinarians consider medical prognosis, treatment burden, and realistic outcomes.

The concept of "doing everything possible" means different things to veterinarians and owners. Owners might interpret this as pursuing all available treatments regardless of the likelihood of outcome. At the same time, veterinarians consider what interventions are most likely to improve or maintain the quality of life.

## **Bridging the Gap: Collaborative Assessment**

The most effective quality of life assessment combines veterinary expertise with owner insight, creating a comprehensive evaluation that considers both clinical indicators and the individual experience of terminal pets within their family environment.

### **Structured Communication Tools**

Many veterinary practices now use structured communication tools that help owners provide relevant information about their pet's daily experience while ensuring that important clinical factors are addressed. Quality of life questionnaires completed before appointments allow owners to reflect on their pet's condition systematically rather than relying solely on impressions during stressful veterinary visits.

Regular quality of life assessments using standardized tools create baseline measurements and track changes over time, providing objective data that both veterinarians and owners can reference during decision-making discussions.

### **Education and Ongoing Dialogue**

Veterinary professionals play a crucial role in educating pet owners about what to observe and how to interpret their pet's behaviors within the context of their medical condition. Understanding typical disease progression patterns helps owners prepare for changes in their pet's condition and recognize when quality of life may be declining.

Quality of life assessment should be an ongoing dialogue rather than a single evaluation, particularly for pets with chronic conditions or age-related decline. This ongoing process includes scheduled quality of life discussions during routine appointments and emergency protocols for situations where quality of life might be rapidly declining.

## **The Quality of Life Conversation**

When significant quality of life concerns arise, the conversation between veterinarians and pet owners becomes particularly crucial. These discussions require sensitivity, clear communication, and respect for both clinical expertise and emotional bonds that families share with their beloved pets. Effective quality of life conversations acknowledge both the veterinary perspective on medical prognosis and treatment options, as well as the owner's intimate knowledge of their pet's personality and preferences, especially in cases involving terminal pets.

These discussions often involve exploring treatment goals and expectations, discussing realistic outcomes for various intervention options, and addressing concerns about pain management and supportive care. Quality of life conversations may also include planning for multiple scenarios, including what indicators might suggest that end-of-life care, such as house call euthanasia services, should be considered. Additionally, it is essential to explore the available options for ensuring the pet's comfort during their final days or weeks.

Ultimately, the goal is to provide a compassionate approach that honors the wishes of both the pet and the owner, ensuring that the right decision is made at the right time. This may involve a hospice plan or a quality of life evaluation to assess the best course of action, allowing for a dignified farewell for beloved dogs or cats.

## **Making the Most Compassionate Choice: When It's Time to Say Goodbye**

When quality of life assessment indicates that a pet's suffering outweighs their enjoyment of life—regardless of whether this conclusion comes from veterinary evaluation, owner observation, or collaborative assessment—the most compassionate choice may be to consider euthanasia. This decision represents the ultimate expression of love and responsibility in the human-animal bond.

The timing of this decision often involves balancing clinical indicators of decline with the pet's individual experience and the family's emotional readiness. Many families find that tracking their pet's good and bad days provides objective information that helps guide this difficult decision. When bad days consistently outnumber good days, or when a pet can no longer engage in activities that previously brought them happiness, it may be time to consider end-of-life options.

In-home euthanasia services like CodaPet provide a peaceful, familiar environment for pets' final moments, allowing families to say goodbye in the comfort of their own space. This option can be particularly meaningful when quality of life assessment has been an ongoing process involving both veterinary guidance and family observation.

The grief that follows these decisions is profound and valid, regardless of whether the choice aligned more closely with veterinary recommendations or owner preferences. Remember that choosing euthanasia when your pet's suffering outweighs their joy represents the ultimate act of love, ensuring their final memory is one of peace and dignity.

## **Conclusion**

Quality of life assessment represents a complex process that benefits from both veterinary expertise and owner insight. Understanding how these different perspectives contribute to comprehensive evaluation can improve communication between pet families and their veterinary teams, leading to better decision-making during challenging health situations.

The most effective quality of life assessment combines veterinary clinical training and objective assessment tools with owners' intimate knowledge of their companion's personality and daily experience. When concerns arise about your pet's well-being, don't hesitate to discuss quality of life assessment with your veterinary team, sharing your observations and concerns while seeking their clinical expertise and guidance.

## **Frequently Asked Questions**

### **Why might my veterinarian recommend euthanasia when I think my pet still has good quality of life?**

Veterinarians are trained to recognize subtle pain indicators and understand disease progression patterns that may not be apparent to pet owners. They also consider future suffering and treatment burden rather than focusing solely on current status, which may lead to earlier recommendations than owners expect, especially for terminal pets.

### **How can I better assess my pet's quality of life at home?**

Keep a simple daily journal to track your pet's appetite, activity level, social interaction, and apparent comfort, noting both good and difficult days. Pay attention to whether your beloved dog still engages in activities they previously enjoyed and can maintain basic functions like feeding, drinking, and elimination without significant difficulty.

### **What should I do if I disagree with my veterinarian's quality of life assessment?**

Ask your veterinarian to explain the specific clinical indicators they're observing and share your detailed observations about your beloved pet's daily experience. Consider seeking a second opinion from another veterinary professional, as both perspectives are valuable for making the right decision regarding your pet's quality of life.
